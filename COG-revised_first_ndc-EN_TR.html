INTRODUCTION

The Paris Climate Agreement, in its article 6, paragraph 1, encourages countries to act voluntarily in consultation in the implementation of their Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs), at:

(i) raise the level of ambition of their mitigation and adaptation measures and (ii) promote the sustainable development and environmental integrity.

The revised NDC of the Republic of Congo is based on its five (05) pillars which relate to: governance, mitigation, adaptation, MRV and financing, was developed following an inclusive process and transparent.

The main strategic axes taken into account to update the initial NDC of 2015 and enhance the country's ambitions in the fight against climate change relate to:

  • strengthening political will and buy-in from national stakeholders and development partners;

  • reviewing, aligning and updating the objectives, policies and measures established in the areas of climate and sustainable development;

  • the integration of new sectors and / or greenhouse gases in the revised NDC;

  • evaluation of the costs and investment possibilities of the priority actions selected in the fields of climate and sustainable development;

  • monitoring progress and enhancing transparency.

I. CONTEXT

The Republic of the Congo which covers an area of ​​342,000 km² is located in Central Africa, straddling the equator between Gabon, the Atlantic Ocean, Cameroon, the Central African Republic, the Democratic Republic of the Congo and the Angolan enclave of Cabinda.

Figure 1: Administrative map of the Republic of Congo

The Congolese economy is mainly based on the mining and extractive industries (oil and gas) and the primary sector (agriculture, fishing and forestry). The population will go from 3,697,490 inhabitants in 2007 to 6,560,384 inhabitants in 2030. Women, who constitute 50.7% of the population (RGPH, 2007), will still be in the majority in terms of numbers. The population of the Republic of Congo is among the most vulnerable, insofar as it has limited room for adaptation, mainly due to poverty. Maintaining the services provided by natural ecosystems (forests, savannas, hydrological basins, etc.) is essential to ensure future development relays, limit the impacts of climate change and offer possibilities for adaptation to the most vulnerable groups, including are part of women and young people of all socio-cultural categories of urban and rural centers. The economic sectors which serve as the basis of socio-economic development of the Republic of Congo are: energy, transport, industry, mines, agriculture, forestry, water, tourism, trade; human settlements and health, coastal landscape, waste, etc.

II. MITIGATION

The greenhouse gases taken into account are:

  • Carbon dioxide (CO2),

  • Methane (CH4),

  • Nitrous oxide (N2O),

  • F gases such as Hydrofluorocarbon (HFC), Perfluorinated Hydrocarbons (PFC), Sulfur hexafluoride (SF6)

    The GHG emitting sectors taken into account in the revised NDC of the Republic of Congo are:

  • Energy, for emissions from the energy industries, manufacturing and construction industries, transportation, residences, commerce, solid fuels, oil and natural gas;

  • Agriculture, Forestry and other Land Use (AFAT) with emissions from enteric fermentation, manure management in animal husbandry, rice cultivation, agricultural soils, burnt savannas and burnt agricultural residues, emissions and removals from forests, etc. ;

  • Industrial processes and product use (PIUP), for emissions from mining industries (cement, lime, glass, etc.), chemical industries (ammonia and other acids), metallurgical industries (iron, steel, lead, aluminum, etc.) and other industrial productions (electrical and electrical equipment, solvent, aerosol, etc.);

  • Waste with solid and liquid waste emissions.

III. ADAPTATION

In accordance with Articles 7.10 and 7.11 of the Paris Agreement, the Republic of Congo has chosen to integrate an adaptation component, which will include its priorities, implementation and support needs, plans and his actions.

3.1- Impacts, risks and vulnerabilities

The current NDC aims to accelerate the socio-economic growth of Congo by comprehensively addressing specific vulnerabilities in the sector and by unlocking and directing national and external investments towards adaptation for effective climate action.

3.1.1- Energy Sector

a) - Wood energy subsector

The impacts of climate change on the biomass-energy sub-sector noted by the populations are among others the depletion of wood-energy resources, the remoteness of the wood-energy collection areas, the increase in the price of wood energy. In the future, if nothing is done and considering the impact of climate change according to the RCP6.0 scenario, the global vulnerability index of this sub-sector will increase slightly due to the increasingly high demand. , depletion of resources and its exposure.

b) - Hydropower sub-sector

The potential of hydroelectricity is strongly linked to the availability of water resources, especially surface water resources, and their inlet flows at the level of the basins where the dams are installed. However, water resources constitute one of the sectors most exposed to climate change due to their dependence on the climate, in particular the variation of several climatic parameters among which are rainfall, evaporation. and temperature. Analyzes indicate that the hydropower sub-sector is moderately exposed to climatic hazards. The main hazards are the poor distribution of rains and drought.

c) - Hydrocarbons sub-sector

The reduction in the energy supply in biomass and hydroelectricity should increase the consumption of hydrocarbons. As the trend of the increase in hydrocarbon consumption may be a foreseeable situation on a large scale (sub-region, continent, etc.), an increasingly important imbalance of supply and demand should lead to an increase in prices. petroleum products on the world market. The marked increase in the consumption of hydrocarbons (gasoline, diesel) in the Congo in response to the energy crisis from 1998 (beginning of the years of extreme droughts), is a situation which justifies the trend of recourse to petroleum products in the event of major hydroelectric power deficit in Congo.

3.1.2- Agriculture, Forestry and other Land Use Sector (AFAT)

a) Vulnerability and impacts of climate change on the agriculture sub-sector

Climate change impacts the Congolese agricultural sub-sector to varying degrees depending on the region considered. However, certain climatic factors such as the increase in the extent of the dry spells, the disruption of the seasons, the irregularity of the rains, the decrease in annual precipitation, floods, the increase in temperatures, etc., pose a serious threat to the environment. this sub-sector in Congo.

b) Vulnerability and impacts of climate change on the forestry and other land use sub-sector

The following climatic factors pose a serious threat to the forestry sub-sector in the Republic of Congo. These are: the increase in the extent of dry spells, the disruption of the seasons, the shift in the seasons, the irregularity of the rains, the intense drought, the decrease in annual precipitation and the increase in temperatures.

3.1.3- Water Resources Sector

The most vulnerable groundwater resources are those of the coastal basin, because they are used to supply drinking water to the agglomeration of Pointe-Noire, which is constantly developing economically with strong population growth. The superficial layer is fed thickly by effective average infiltration and it is very stressed by boreholes and many undeclared traditional wells.

The increasingly severe low water levels over the past 25 years (because the average value of minimum heights has fallen by 157%), to which is added an aggravation of the phenomenon of silting up, have serious consequences on biodiversity, fishing and navigation with a clear decline in traffic at the port of Brazzaville.

3.1.4- Human establishments and health

By 2020, 2050, 2080 and 2100, with the combination of soil modification by paving, thermal activities (power plants, transport, etc.), we can expect an artificial warming in cities of this size. The reduction in green spaces (increase in the albedo effect and the absence of water bodies) also contributes to the modification of the thermal and pluviometric balance.

Congo's demographic and health indicators highlight the worrying state of the population's health. This state is characterized by high maternal neonatal, infant and child mortality and high morbidity.

Climate projections as planned will increase these endemic situations with low sanitation capacity and chronic malnutrition.

3.1.5- Coastal zone

The relevant hazards identified in the coastal zone of Congo are: coastal erosion, floods and floods, marine submersion, late rains, pockets of drought, sea level rise, strong winds, salinization and degradation of system humidities lagoon. The major element of effect and scale known on the Congolese coast is marine erosion which is distinguished by a decline in the coastline. The rise in sea level is still hardly noticeable.

3.1.6- Tourism

In the tourism sector, the increase in temperature could lead to the migration and / or destruction of fauna leading to a decrease in tourist activity (attendance). The variability of the rain would lead to seasonal unavailability and a decrease in tourist activity.

3.2- Type of adaptation objectives

To strengthen resilience in the face of the adverse effects of climate change, it is envisaged:

  • Protection of populations;

  • The protection of the natural heritage, biodiversity, forests and fishery resources;

  • Buildings;

  • The protection of productive systems sensitive to climate change, such as agriculture;

  • The protection of high-risk infrastructure systems.

The objectives for increasing resilience in the forestry sector include:

  • Stop deforestation and degradation of native forests;

  • Maintain national parks, reserves and protected areas;

  • Creation and management of forest reserves;

  • Promote reforestation and rehabilitation of cleared and degraded forests with tree species resilient to climate change and ecologically and socially appropriate;

  • Promote integrated agroforestry in areas intended for agriculture;

  • Discourage tree felling on tax allowances;

  • Encourage tax allotment holders to plant and manage trees on their properties.

CONCLUSION

The present CDN of the Republic of Congo, which saw the effective participation of national stakeholders and technical and financial partners, was carried out in a context characterized by the economic crisis (with the drastic drop in the prices of raw materials) and the health crisis (with the COVID 19 pandemic).

Despite these unprecedented crises, which drastically reduce the country's financial capacities, the Government and other stakeholders are committed to raising the national ambition in terms of reducing emissions. This commitment should in no way compromise the socio-economic development of the country.

The national stakeholders who commit to implementing the 2021 NDC take to heart the preamble of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), which emphasizes that “the measures taken to deal with climate change must be closely coordinated with social and economic development in order to avoid any negative impacts, taking into account the legitimate priority needs of developing countries, namely sustainable economic growth and poverty eradication. They are also aware that Article 2 of the aforementioned convention states the following: “The ultimate objective of this Convention and of any related legal instruments that the Conference of the Parties may adopt is to stabilize, in accordance with the provisions relevant of the Convention, concentrations of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere to a level that prevents dangerous anthropogenic disturbance of the climate system. This level should be reached in sufficient time for ecosystems to adapt naturally to climate change, for food production not to be threatened and for economic development to continue in a sustainable manner.

The preamble to the Paris Agreement emphasizes that climate change is a matter of concern for all of humanity. Countries should take into account their respective obligations regarding human rights, the right to health, the rights of indigenous peoples, local communities, migrants, children, persons with disabilities, in their actions in the face of climate change. and people in vulnerable situations, and the right to development, as well as gender equality, women's empowerment and intergenerational equity.

It is on these principles that national stakeholders will work to implement the NDC, both in its conditional and unconditional scenarios.
