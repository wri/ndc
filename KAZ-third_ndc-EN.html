Summary

The Republic of Kazakhstan reaffirms its strong commitment to the objectives of the Paris Agreement through the submission of its enhanced Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC 3.0). Building upon previous efforts, NDC 3.0 reflects a significant step-up in national ambition and transparency, aligning Kazakhstan’s development trajectory with the long-term goal of achieving carbon neutrality by 2060.

The NDC raises Kazakhstan’s mitigation target, with national net greenhouse gas emissions set to decline by 17 percent below 1990 levels by 2035 (unconditional) and up to 25 percent with international support.

NDC 3.0 covers the common 2026–2035 time frame and is aligned with the Strategy on Achieving Carbon Neutrality by 2060. It is backed by an updated Environmental Code, a revitalized Emissions Trading System (ETS), and programs on green finance, afforestation, energy efficiency, renewable energy, and climate-resilient infrastructure, with a forthcoming National Adaptation Plan and links to the National Development Plan to 2029.

The target covers all IPCC sectors and gases with emissions and removals estimated by using IPCC 2006 Guidelines with AR5 GWPs.

Under the unconditional pathway, net emissions reach ~328 MtCO₂e in 2030 (15%) and ~320 MtCO₂e (17%) by 2035, driven by a cleaner power mix that lifts the share of renewables and other low-emission sources toward ~35 percent by 2035; with international support, emissions can fall to ~288 MtCO₂e by 2035. The NDC integrates adaptation, loss and damage, and a just transition.

This NDC reflects Kazakhstan’s comprehensive, whole- of-government approach to climate policy, supported by strong inter-ministerial coordination and the engagement of civil society, private sector, and international partners. It operationalizes national climate strategies, including the Strategy on Achieving Carbon Neutrality by 2060 and the Action Plan for the Transition to a Green Economy, by translating long-term goals into measurable sectoral targets and investment priorities.

Progress will be tracked against the most recent reported 1990 net emissions in the Biennial Transparency Report, with any recalculations being applied in line with IPCC good-practice guidance. The document was prepared through an inclusive process involving ministries, sub-national authorities, experts, civil society, the private sector, and international partners.

Kazakhstan recognizes that achieving these targets requires sustained domestic effort and enhanced international cooperation.

Indicative investment needs for 2026–2035 are estimated at about USD 175,6 billion (USD 128.5 billion for mitigation; USD 47 billion for adaptation), or roughly 5.5 percent of current GDP annually; counting only additional costs above BAU, needs are ~USD 71.8 billion (~2.5 percent of GDP annually). Kazakhstan estimates requirements for around USD 20 billion in highly concessional international finance.

Kazakhstan has already financed a large share of climate action domestically through the republican budget, SOEs, and local delivery systems.

The country continues to mobilize national resources while calling for predictable, concessional, and technology-oriented international support to enable deeper decarbonization, accelerate adaptation, and ensure a just and equitable transition.

Through NDC 3.0, Kazakhstan demonstrates its determination to contribute meaningfully to the global effort to limit temperature increase, while ensuring sustainable development and the well-being of present and future generations.

Implementation is underpinned by market and transparency systems, strengthening the ETS with tighter carbon budgets and improved MRV, and enabling participation in Article 6 with clear authorizations, corresponding adjustments, safeguards, and required institutions and systems, while prioritizing just transition and inclusion.

The new NDC of Kazakhstan is all about growth through innovation and modernization and best use of talents of people and natural resource endowment, and not about scarcity and constrains.

Introduction

The Paris Agreement, adopted in 2015, set the global course for limiting temperature rise to well below 2 °C and pursuing efforts to keep it to 1.5 °C, while strengthening adaptive capacity and aligning finance with low-emission, climate-resilient development pathways.

Determined Contributions (NDCs) are the core instruments for achieving these goals and are updated every five years to reflect increasing ambition, in line with countries’ circumstances and the principle of common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities.

The first Global Stocktake (GST), concluded at COP28, provided a comprehensive assessment of collective progress under the Paris Agreement. It underscored the urgency of deep, rapid and sustained reductions in GHG emissions in line with 1.5 °C pathways, strengthening adaptation, and scaling up support for developing economies.

The GST also strengthened calls for transitioning away from fossil fuels in the energy systems, with a particular focus on accelerating efforts towards the phase-down of unabated coal-based electricity and deploying clean energy alternatives in line with national circumstances. It emphasized the importance of conserving, protecting and restoring nature and ecosystems towards achieving the Paris Agreement temperature goal. With regards to adaptation, the GST called for significantly scaling up adaptation efforts, including doubling adaptation finance by 2025, enhancing national planning and implementation, and acknowledged the role of the Global Goal on Adaptation (GGA) to guide countries’ adaptation effort towards enhancing resilience, especially in vulnerable sectors and regions.

Kazakhstan’s NDC 3.0 responds to this global call by enhancing national ambition and aligning future actions with the priorities identified through the stocktake process.

In 2023, the Government adopted the Strategy on Achieving Carbon Neutrality by 2060, signaling a decisive long-term transition toward a diversified, low-emission economy.

This strategy is supported by a strengthened Environmental Code, a revitalized Emissions Trading System, and programs on green finance, afforestation, energy efficiency, renewable energy deployment, and climate-resilient infrastructure.

The forthcoming National Adaptation Plan and the National Development Plan to 2029 will further integrate climate objectives into sectoral and territorial development planning.

Kazakhstan’s Third Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC 3.0) covers the period 2026-2035 and is aligned with the Strategy on Achieving Carbon Neutrality by 2060 (CNS 2060), serving as the next ten-year step toward that long-term goal.

It sets an economy-wide target of:

17 percent below 1990 levels by 2035 (unconditional), and up to 25 percent below 1990 levels by 2035 conditional on international support in finance, technology transfer, and capacity building, while strengthening resilience to negative climate change impacts.

With its NDC 3.0 submission, Kazakhstan raises its mitigation ambition in line with its national circumstances and long- term Strategy on Achieving Carbon Neutrality by 2060, demonstrating commitment to the collective effort and charting a credible pathway to contribute fairly to the global goal of limiting temperature rise while building a resilient, inclusive, and sustainable economy at home.

1.1. National Circumstances

Kazakhstan is the world’s largest landlocked country and an LLDC. Its vast territory, continental climate, and distance to seaports shape logistics costs, energy security, and adaptation needs. Major river systems are transboundary, which makes water availability and allocation a strategic concern for agriculture, industry, and cities. Regional development is uneven, with coal- and mining-dependent oblasts facing distinct transition challenges.

The economy is diversified yet energy and materials intensive. Heavy industry, mining and metals, oil and gas, and transport are major contributors to GDP, exports, and employment.

Power generation relies significantly on coal, with gas and hydropower providing balancing roles. Grid reliability, aging assets, and winter peak constraints influence the pace and sequencing of clean energy integration.

Prolonged droughts in recent years have further reduced crop yields and degraded pastures.

Floods and droughts already threaten lives and livelihoods in Kazakhstan, and climate change is intensifying and shifting these hazards.

As the impacts of climate change deepen, patterns of drought and flooding are likely to change. While formal event attribution for floods in Kazakhstan is still developing, the Government treats flood and drought risk as a primary concern for climate change adaptation, disaster risk reduction, and long-term socio-economic development. Kazakhstan will plan and invest on the basis that climate change is already altering hazard patterns, while continuing to refine attribution with new data.

Kazakhstan’s GHG profile reflects its economic structure. While per capita emissions remain higher than the global average (about 14t CO₂-eq in 2023 due to its historic reliance on coal and energy-intensive industries), the country’s overall contribution to global emissions is less than 0.7 percent. This imbalance, with mounting climate-related losses despite a relatively small share in global emissions, underscores the urgency of pursuing a low-emission, climate-resilient development pathway.

1.3. Challenges in implementing the NDC

The continental climate drives very high winter heating demand and rising summer cooling demand. Conventional air-source heat pumps perform poorly at sub-zero temperatures. Sustainable biomass potential is limited and undeveloped, while expanding domestic natural-gas development is capital-intensive slow.

Hydropower is constrained by variable and declining inflows on transboundary rivers influenced by upstream withdrawals and climate- driven glacial retreat.

Coal therefore remains an important, though carbon-intensive, source of heat and power in the near term. Energy planning also underweights adaptation needs such as peak loads during heatwaves and higher electricity demand for irrigation and cooling.

Although the government is prioritizing renewables and gasification, energy planning often overlooks adaptation needs such as rising cooling and irrigation demand, as well as power-system vulnerability to peak loads during heatwaves.

Socio-economic dependence on coal is significant on several oblasts. Jobs, local budgets and supply chains are tied to coal mining and coal-based power. A just transition framework is nascent, and practical guidance for reskilling workers, diversifying local economies, and protecting affected households remain limited.

Carbon pricing reforms are essential for decarbonization but could raise energy and transport costs if not carefully sequenced and paired with targeted social protection.

Financial conditions remain a significant obstacle. Green investments face high capital costs compared to fossil fuel- based projects due to low end-user tariffs, persistent fossil fuel subsidies, and limited access to affordable long-term financing. Innovative equipment is often not accepted as collateral, limiting borrowing options.

1.4. Development process

The preparation of Kazakhstan’s NDC 3.0 was guided at the highest political level. By Order No. 144 of 11 June 2025, a National Working Group on NDC 3.0, chaired by the First Deputy Prime Minister, was established as the main decision-making and coordination platform.

The process was coordinated with three national initiatives in parallel:

the Roadmap for the Strategy on Achieving Carbon Neutrality by 2060, the drafting of the National Adaptation Plan, and the update of the Concept for Transition to a Green Economy under Presidential oversight.

This ensured that targets, measures and investment signals are consistent across instruments and guided by a shared long-term vision for decarbonization, resilience and sustainable development. The Working Group also considered the first Global Stocktake and relevant CMA decisions.

The Group included representatives from all key ministries and agencies for energy, industry, agriculture, water resources, finance, economy and others, as well as state-owned enterprises, business associations, academia and civil-society organizations.

Robust technical work underpinned the NDC development. Over 2024– 2025, sector teams drew on the national GHG inventory and the Biennial Transparency Report to update baselines, test mitigation and adaptation options, and assess implementation pathways.

Analysis covered energy system transitions, methane abatement, indus- trial efficiency, land use and agriculture, water management, and just transition considerations, with attention to sequencing, grid integration, and investment needs. Broad and inclusive consultations complemented the technical work.

Particular attention was paid to gender issues and the involvement of vulnerable groups, including youth and children. Youth organizations participated in technical meetings and stakeholder roundtables alongside line ministries, experts and regional authorities. NDC 3.0 establishes a systemic approach to protecting the rights of children and youth in the context of climate change.

This combination of political leadership coordinated national planning, technical evidence, and inclusive participation has produced an NDC 3.0 that is coherent, forward-looking and aligned with the Strategy on Achieving Carbon Neutrality by 2060. It provides a credible foundation for implementation, resource mobilization and transparent monitoring of progress.

2.1. Policies related to mitigation

Over the past decade, Kazakhstan has built a more coherent policy and legislative framework to guide the low-carbon transition and align national development with global climate objectives.

Strategic Vision for Decarbonisation

This framework combines long-term strategies, medium-term development plans, regulatory reforms and market-based instruments, providing a foundation for achieving the targets set in the NDC 3.0 and the national goal of carbon neutrality by 2060.

Kazakhstan’s long-term climate policy is guided by the Strategy on Achieving Carbon Neutrality by 2060 , adopted in 2023.

The Strategy defines the national decarbonisation trajectory, outlining sectoral pathways and investment needs estimated at USD 610 billion to 2060. It calls for redirecting existing capital flows toward green technologies and mobilising new public and private investment.

Medium-Term Development and Green Transition Plans

A detailed Roadmap is being developed to guide implementation and sectoral sequencing.

The National Development Plan to 2029 (adopted 2024) establishes priorities for 2025–2029, promoting diversification, innovation, and human-capital development as enablers of a just, low-emission transition.

The Action Plan for Transition to a Green Economy (2024–2030) complements this by setting concrete green-finance and sustainable- investment targets, including increased issuance of green loans and bonds by 2030.

Together, these plans connect Kazakhstan’s long-term carbon-neutral vision with near-term policy and investment priorities.

Carbon Pricing

Kazakhstan operates Central Asia’s first ETS , initially launched in 2013 and reformed in 2018. The system covers about 42.9 % of national CO₂ emissions from 212 large installations in the energy, oil and gas, mining, metallurgy, chemical, and construction-materials sectors.

The ETS provides a platform to strengthen price signals and stimulate low carbon as allocation coverage, and MRV are upgraded. The national carbon budget for covered sectors is being reduced by 3.41% annually through 2030, consistent with recent reforms.

Legal and Regulatory Foundations

The Environmental Code (2021) provides the legal basis for climate action, introducing emission-reduction principles, climate-related permitting, and green-finance mechanisms. It underpins the National Emissions Trading System (ETS) and mandates a gradual decline in the national carbon budget, with revenues from allowance payments channeled toward environmental projects.

Policy Coherence and Implementation

These complementary strategies, plans and instruments together constitute the backbone of Kazakhstan’s climate-policy architecture. The long-term Strategy on Achieving Carbon Neutrality by 2060 defines the destination, the medium-term plans translate it into investment priorities, the Environmental Code provides the legal foundation, and the ETS introduces a market signal for reducing emissions.

For the NDC 3.0, these instruments serve not merely as background policies but as direct enablers of mitigation outcomes. They create incentives for technological modernization, establish a framework for mobilizing public and private finance, and guide sectoral transformation in a way that is consistent with Kazakhstan’s sustainable-development agenda and the principles of a just transition.

2.2 National GHG Emissions (1990–2023)

Kazakhstan’s greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions have undergone marked shifts since 1990, reflecting the country’s profound economic transitions and structural changes in the energy system. In 1990, during the final years of the Soviet-era industrial economy, net GHG emissions stood at about 385 Mt CO₂-equivalent , driven by energy-intensive heavy industry and a strong reliance on fossil fuels. After a rapid decline in the 1990s, emissions stabilized and then began to grow again in the early 2000s. According to the most recent national inventory, net emissions reached about 349.2 Mt CO₂-equivalent in 2023.

Recently, Kazakhstan’s total GHG emissions rose in 2023 compared to 2021, driven by post-COVID economic recovery and increased energy use.

However, total net emissions in 2023 remained 9.4% below 1990 levels.

The energy sector remains the dominant source of emissions, contributing 79.8% (278.5 MtCO₂e) of the total in 2023, with energy industries alone accounting for about 40 % of national net emissions.

The agriculture sector was the second-largest source in 2023, emitting 33.4 Mt CO₂e (9.6%), 20% lower than 1990 and 13.4% below 2021, mainly reflecting livestock dynamics. The IPPU sector contributed 26.7Mt CO₂e (7.7%), up 17.7% since 1990 and 0.8% from 2021.

Waste emissions reached 7.1 Mt CO₂e (2%), a 73.5% increase since 1990, linked to population growth and rising waste volumes.

The LULUCF sector, once a net sink, became a net source with 3.3 Mt CO₂e in 2023, though emissions declined 28.9% from 2021, indicating improved land management.

2.3 Emissions pathways and modelling approach

Business-as-Usual (BAU). A reference scenario if no additional mitigation beyond policies and measures in place at the modeling cutoff date. BAU is a comparator, not a policy target.

Unconditional Mitigation. A policy trajectory reflecting measures adopted and domestically implementable within current institutions and budget frameworks, consistent with national strategies and sectoral plans.

Conditional Mitigation. A higher-ambition trajectory that assumes timely international support, including finance, technology transfer, and capacity building, enabling deeper reductions.

Kazakhstan’s NDC 3.0 is underpinned by three modeled scenarios that reflect different levels of policy effort and access to support. Assumptions on scope, methods and cutoff dates are set out in Annex 1 (ICTU).

These scenarios provide the analytical foundation for assessing Kazakhstan’s mitigation ambition and are used to quantify emission trajectories presented in the following section on GHG emissions under the BAU scenario.

2.3.1. GHG emissions under the BAU scenario

Under the baseline scenario for greenhouse gas emission projections, assumptions were made to reflect the expected trends in socio-economic development and demographic dynamics.

According to the forecast, Kazakhstan’s economy is expected to grow at an average annual rate of 5.6% during 2025–2030 and 3.1% during 2030–2035.

By 2035 , Kazakhstan’s GDP is projected to reach approximately USD 350 billion , driven primarily by growth in industry and the services sector, accompanied by a steady increase in energy demand. Despite gradual improvements in energy efficiency, these gains are insufficient to offset the effects of economic expansion and the continued reliance on coal for electricity and heat production. In the absence of additional measures to curb methane emissions, further growth in emissions is expected from the oil and gas sector and the waste management sector.

The country’s population is also projected to continue increasing, with an average annual growth rate of 0.87% and 0.74%, respectively.

Efficiency gains occur but are insufficient to offset higher activity and continued reliance on coal in power and heat. Without new policies to curb methane, emissions from oil and gas operations and waste also increase. 2.4. Mitigation commitments

Kazakhstan commits to reduce economy-wide net greenhouse gas emissions, including LULUCF, by 17 percent below 1990 levels by 2035 as an unconditional target, and up to 25 percent below 1990 by 2035 conditional on timely international finance, technology transfer, and capacity-building. The targets adopt the 2026–2035 common time frame, cover all IPCC sectors and gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, HFCs, PFCs, SF₆, NF₃), and apply IPCC 2006 Guidelines with AR5 GWPs.

This represents progression from NDC 2.0 and aligns with the Strategy on Achieving Carbon Neutrality by 2060. Ambition will be delivered through domestic policies already in force, and the conditional component will be achieved through international partnerships that lower costs while safeguarding energy security and a just transition.

2.5. Measures to mitigate GHG emissions

Kazakhstan’s NDC 3.0 mitigation strategy is built around a package of priority unconditional measures across all major emitting sectors. These measures are designed to accelerate the country’s energy transition, strengthen energy efficiency, promote low-emission technologies in transport, industry and agriculture, scale up nature-based solutions and improve waste management. In addition, the mitigation strategy places emphasis on reducing short-lived climate pollutants, including methane and black carbon, and on advancing emerging technologies such as hydrogen, carbon capture, and other innovative low-carbon solutions. Taken together, they enable the achievement of the unconditional mitigation pathway and provide the foundation for deeper cuts under the conditional pathway, in line with the national Strategy on Achieving Carbon Neutrality by 2060.

Energy, including Power Sector

Ensuring a reliable and balanced energy transition, Kazakhstan will expand its low-carbon and transition generation capacity, bringing its share of total electricity generation to at least 35% by 2035 from renewable and nuclear energy sources.

The use of transition fuels will be carefully balanced to support cleaner heating - reducing particulate matter and black-carbon emissions - while enhancing power-system flexibility to integrate renewable energy and meet growing demand from the transport and industrial sectors

Deployment of Best Available Technologies in power and heat generation, and continued fuel switch of residential areas will contribute to reduction of particulate emissions and black carbon.

Power plants, heating networks, and utility systems will be modernized to replace outdated infrastructure along with automated monitoring systems to reduce wear across electricity, heat, water, and wastewater networks.

Efficiency of energy use will improve through Smart Grid and Smart Metering technologies, reactive power compensation, and the transition to higher voltage levels to support electrification and integration of electric vehicles.

Tariff and financing reforms will gradually reduce cross-subsidies, while social protection measures will help low-income households by capping utility expenses at 10% of income.

To stimulate a low-carbon transition across the economy, Kazakhstan will gradually strengthen the emission cap for covered industries, providing a predictable price signal for investment in low-carbon technologies within the ETS. The development of the ETS will create the foundation for further interaction with other market mechanisms and the attraction of international climate finance.

To improve transparency, coherence, and investment attraction, Kazakhstan will establish a Country Platform for Climate Finance Mobilization – a coordination mechanism bringing together government agencies, the private sector, and international partners.

The platform will ensure synergies between the ETS, the implementation of Article 6 of the Paris Agreement, the Carbon Fund, and enterprise transition plans, creating a unified framework for the preparation, financing, and monitoring of climate projects, as well as the development of a portfolio of bankable initiatives using blended finance instruments.

Short-Lived Climate Pollutants

Pilot projects will be implemented through international partnerships, climate finance programs, and results-based mechanisms, using revenues from carbon transactions to demonstrate and implement innovative technologies, develop competencies, and scale up solutions in high-emitting sectors.

Kazakhstan plans to introduce a system of state regulation of methane emissions, including the development of bylaws, monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) rules, and the creation of a National Methane Emissions Database and its integration with the state carbon inventory.

Additional measures to reduce short-lived climate pollutants include the implementation of a National Methane Emissions Reduction Program, which establishes priority sectors, targets, and deadlines for achievement, with mandatory inventory and annual reporting by regulated entities.

Methane leaks will be reduced through the implementation of leak detection and response (LDAR) systems, equipment modernization, and the establishment of flaring efficiency requirements (at least 98%), with flaring considered a preferable option over direct gas emissions.

Furthermore, institutional and legal regulation of methane will be strengthened, including the harmonization of national regulations with the European Union Methane Regulation and the Global Methane Commitment. It’s worth noting that many of the measures included in the NDCs will also reduce short-lived climate pollutants (SLCPs). Modernizing the thermal power industry and switching to cleaner fuels will reduce emissions of black carbon and particulate matter.

Expanding energy efficiency programs and developing public transportation will reduce urban air pollution. Implementing methane capture and use systems at industrial facilities, landfills, and in agriculture will reduce emissions of methane—the most significant SLCP. At the same time, waste management measures and improved manure management will reduce both methane and nitrous oxide.

Taken together, these measures will not only contribute to achieving climate goals but also deliver tangible social and environmental benefits, including improved air quality, public health, and resilience to climate risks.

2.6 Socio-economic considerations

A just and inclusive transition

Kazakhstan will manage the social and economic implications of decarbonization, so mitigation is feasible, fair, and investable. The Government will protect vulnerable groups, support workers and regions in transition, and expand participation and opportunity as new sectors grow.

Kazakhstan’s transition to a low-emission economy will require gradual decline in coal and other fossil fuels, especially in power generation and heating. This transition brings important social and economic challenges for coal-dependent regions, where local budgets, jobs and infrastructure have long relied on the coal industry. To ensure that no region or community is left behind, the Government will pursue a just transition that:

  • assessing local labour markets and skills,

  • supporting re-training and re-deployment of workers,

  • diversifying regional economies, and

  • providing social protection for affected households.

Gender-responsive mitigation

Women and men experience climate risks and the transition differently due to unequal access to resources, land, finance, and decision-making.

Women play central roles in household energy, water management, and small-scale farming, making them indispensable partners in the shift to low-emission and climate-resilient practices.

Kazakhstan has made progress by embedding gender considerations into national policies and launching initiatives such as the Women for Just Transition Network.

Government aims to:

mainstream gender considerations in climate-related laws, strategies and investment plans, expand women’s participation in decision-making and access to finance, and promote capacity- building for women in sectors such as renewable energy, sustainable agriculture and land management.

Engaging youth and safeguarding children

Gender-responsive climate policy will not only make mitigation more effective but also promote fairer distribution of opportunities and benefits.

Children and youth are both vulnerable to climate risks and key agents of change in the transformation toward a green economy.

Kazakhstan’s NDC 3.0 reflects this dual perspective by:

integrating climate-resilient design in schools, healthcare and public services, promoting climate education and expanding youth participation in environmental initiatives, creating financing windows for youth-led projects and green entrepreneurship, and investing in community-based resilience that benefits families and future generations.

These measures ensure that the transition to a low-emission future also protects children’s rights, strengthens their opportunities, and equips young people to be partners in delivering Kazakhstan’s climate commitments.

They will be tracked through NDC implementation and just transition monitoring, with disaggregated indicators where feasible.

CLIMATE CHANGE ADAPTATION

3.1. Kazakhstan’s efforts and achievements in climate change adaptation

3.1.1. Adaptation related policies and plans

Kazakhstan’s adaptation policy is shaped by a comprehensive legal and strategic framework that integrates climate risk considerations into national development and sectoral planning.

The Environmental Code (2021) mandates systematic vulnerability assessments, sectoral adaptation plans, prioritizing water management, agriculture, forestry, and emergency preparedness.

The Water Code (2025) includes a separate chapter dedicated to adaptation issues, which provides for the integration of climate risks into water resources management and planning. The Code establishes principles for assessing the impact of climate change on water resources, ensuring the sustainable use of surface and groundwater, and prioritizing water supply to critical sectors during periods of water scarcity.

Sectoral strategies translate these policies and plans into concrete action:

The Comprehensive plan for the development of the water sector for 2024–2028 (2024) prioritizes efficiency, modern irrigation, and infrastructure resilience.

The Concept for the Development of the Agro-Industrial Complex for 2021-2030 (2021) promotes climate-smart farming and water-saving crops.

The Concept of Transition to a Green Economy (2013, updated 2024) expands ecosystem-based approaches, restoration of degraded land, and sustainable water and soil management.

These policies provide a foundation but also reveal where integration across sectors is still limited.

3.1.2. Adaptation measures and current efforts

Kazakhstan’s adaptation actions focus largely on technical and infrastructure-driven measures, especially in water, agriculture, and disaster risk reduction. These include, among others:

Increasing water storage capacity to 2.65 billion m³ by 2028, reducing irrigation losses from 50 % to 35 %, modernizing canals, and strengthening hydrological monitoring (Ministry of Water Resources and Irrigation, 2024).

Scaling up climate-smart agriculture, diversifying away from water- intensive crops, promoting organic farming, and restoring 1.5 million ha of degraded land through afforestation and reforestation under the Bonn Challenge by 2030 (Ministry of Agriculture, 2021; Ministry of Ecology and Natural Resources, 2023).

  • Reinforcing flood and mudflow protection, de-risking moraine lakes, and expanding early-warning and monitoring systems (Ministry of Emergency Situations, 2023).

  • Development and implementation of unified national rules for all regions on population evacuation (routes, transport, temporary accommodation) and protection of critical infrastructure facilities (energy, water supply, transport) in the context of climate-induced emergencies (Ministry for Emergency Situations, 2023).

  • Expansion of the hydrological monitoring network on small and medium-sized rivers to improve the accuracy of flood forecasting and enhance modelling systems (Ministry for Emergency Situations).

  • Development and deployment of digital systems and specialized software for modelling and forecasting the consequences of climate- induced emergencies, with subsequent integration into the national risk management system (Ministry for Emergency Situations).

  • Equipping civil protection units with modern monitoring and response tools, including unmanned aerial vehicles, satellite observation systems, and mobile early-warning complexes (Ministry for Emergency Situations).

  • Advancing digitalized epidemiological surveillance and creating a national forecasting and biosafety system by 2026 (Ministry of Healthcare, 2022).

Kazakhstan recognizes that protecting and restoring freshwater wetlands – rivers, lakes, and peatlands – delivers co-benefits for both mitigation and adaptation: storing carbon while strengthening resilience of water, food, and biodiversity systems.

As part of its NDC, Kazakhstan will implement measures to protect, restore, and sustainably manage wetlands, with quantitative targets to be reflected in future NDC and NAP updates.

Kazakhstan became a member of the Freshwater Challenge in December 2024, which will further enhance this process by reinforcing national commitments to climate and nature through a global partnership.

3.2. Climate change projections

The analysis focused on two plausible future scenarios: RCP 4.5 and RCP 7.0. The analysis selected two scenarios to provide the opportunity to assess impacts and the likely efficacy of adaptation measures across a range of plausible futures.

Applying climate projections to adaptation planning requires developing plausible scenarios of future change and disaggregating trends by geography and season to capture the complex pathways through which climate impacts can affect human well-being and the larger national economy.

3.2.1. Projected changes in temperature and precipitation

Various reports conclude that surface air temperatures in Kazakhstan will rise through 2100.

Climate scenarios do not diverge greatly by mid-century, suggesting warming of between one- and two-degrees C. After mid-century, the two scenarios evaluated for this NDC (RCP 4.5 and RCP 7.0) diverged significantly, with the RCP 7.0 scenario reaching over 1.5 °C warmer than the RCP 4.5 scenario by the end of the century. Warming will be most pronounced in the summer, a principal agricultural season in Kazakhstan, and the greatest warming will be experienced in the north and northeastern regions of the country where most grain is cultivated.

Kazakhstan is also projected to experience an increasing number of extreme heat days across the century. By mid-century, this increase is likely to both impact grain production and stress the health of vulnerable members of the population, including children and the elderly, particularly in urban areas and Oblasts with extended periods of high heat.

While Kazakhstan is projected to see increases in annual precipitation, in the summer, parts of the country will see seasonal declines in precipitation.

Under RCP 4.5, these decreases are concentrated in the northern and eastern parts of the country, particularly in some regions responsible for wheat production. Under RCP 7.0, decreases spread more broadly across the northeast and eastern parts of the country in the second half of the 21st Century.

Mountain regions in the eastern part of the country may initially face increased flood risk from enhanced river flows due to snowmelt, but this risk could diminish later in the century as snowpack shrinks and shifts to rain-dominated runoff. Heatwaves, however, are likely to become more frequent and prolonged under both emissions scenarios.

    LOSS AND DAMAGE

    4.1. Overview of loss and damage

    As a vast, land-locked developing country exposed to increasing climate extremes, Kazakhstan is already experiencing severe impacts, including devastating floods, wildfires, droughts, and record-breaking heat that damage infrastructure, disrupt food and energy systems, and threaten lives and livelihoods.

    The Government recognizes that these climate-related challenges demand not only stronger mitigation and adaptation efforts but also dedicated measures to avert, minimize, and address loss and damage (L&D).

    The country’s participation as an alternate member of the Fund for Responding Loss&Damage reflects its intention to strengthen access to international technical assistance and finance while bringing Central Asia’s unique challenges to global attention.

    Kazakhstan is committed to protecting the most vulnerable populations, including low-income households, women, children, elderly persons, persons with disabilities, and farmers, whose livelihoods are most at risk. Kazakhstan recognizes the mobility consequences of climate impacts, including temporary and permanent internal displacement, and planned relocation.

    L&D in Kazakhstan stems from both sudden-onset hazards – floods, droughts, wildfires, dust storms, heatwaves, and mudslides, and slow-onset processes such as rising temperatures, shifting precipitation patterns, land degradation, soil salinization, water scarcity, and biodiversity loss.

    These impacts cause both economic losses, including infrastructure damage, agricultural decline, and business disruption, and non-economic losses, such as loss of lives, ecosystems, cultural heritage, and traditional knowledge, many of which are irreplaceable.

    Going forward, Kazakhstan intends to strengthen existing efforts to minimize the impacts of floods, droughts, fires and other emergencies, while integrating loss and damage issues into national planning.

    4.2.2. Non-economic losses and damages

    Non-economic harms are wide-ranging, from deterioration of health and displacement to loss of cultural identity and ecosystem services.

    In western Kazakhstan, repeated floods have caused permanent migration, while extreme heat and water shortages in Mangystau have triggered psychological distress and health impact.

    In the Turkistan region, water scarcity has eroded traditional farming livelihoods and driven state-led relocation.

    The desiccation of the Aral Sea, while largely human-induced, has led to collapsed livelihoods, biodiversity decline, and extensive health impacts, and is now compounded by climate change through intensified dust storms and salinization.

    These are but a few examples of the ongoing climate-related impacts facing the country.

    Vulnerable groups, including women, children, older persons, persons with disabilities, and low-income households, face disproportionate impacts due to limited adaptive capacity and heightened exposure.

    While these harms are not easily quantifiable, they are reflective of enduring dimensions of Kazakhstan’s L&D.

  • Enabling legal framework for addressing loss and damage

    Kazakhstan’s existing legislation provides a strong foundation for integrating climate-related loss and damage considerations into national planning and response.

    A number of sectoral laws and codes already address issues relevant to climate risk management.

    Key instruments include:

    The Environmental Code (2021), which supports biodiversity conservation and climate adaptation;

    the Code on Public Health and the Healthcare System (2020), which recognizes environmental health risks;

    the Law on Civil Protection (2014), which establishes a comprehensive national framework for emergency preparedness and disaster response.

    Complementary provisions in the Water Code, Land Code, Forest Code, and the Law on Cultural Heritage further reinforce resilience by providing frameworks for the sustainable management of natural resources and the protection of cultural assets.

    These legal foundations, together with Kazakhstan’s international commitments under the Paris Agreement and related frameworks, create the basis for strengthening climate-specific L&D provisions, including recognition of non-economic dimensions such as impacts on ecosystems, cultural heritage, and public health. Future work will continue to build on these instruments to ensure an integrated approach to addressing both sudden- and slow-onset climate-related losses.

    4.3.1. Social protection

    Kazakhstan has put in place a range of social protection mechanisms that help households and communities recover from climate-related disasters.

    These include:

    • emergency food and cash assistance,

    • temporary housing support for displaced populations,

    • agricultural insurance schemes and

    • loan deferral programs to support farmers affected by extreme weather events.

    Preparedness and response are further supported by the Comprehensive Civil Protection Priority Action Plan for 2023–2027.

    These measures contribute to reducing vulnerability and improving recovery outcomes for affected populations.

    Ongoing efforts aim to further align social protection systems with climate-related challenges, ensuring that support is timely, inclusive, and accessible to the most affected groups.

    4.3.2. Addressing loss and damage for climate-displaced communities

    According to international assessments, climate-related internal migration in Kazakhstan could affect between 3.1–4.6% of the projected population (IOM 2023). Building on current experience, Kazakhstan will develop subsidiary legislation and guidance for planned relocation as a last resort, to ensure that relocation processes are planned in a participatory way that safeguards the rights, livelihoods, and cultural heritage of affected communities.

    Climate-related displacement is already a recognized phenomenon in Kazakhstan. Resettlement initiatives have supported communities in moving from regions facing acute water scarcity and environmental pressures in the south to more secure areas in the north and east.

    Integrating climate mobility considerations into adaptation and disaster risk-reduction strategies is an important focus area.

    This approach supports long-term resilience and enhances the participation of relocated populations in decision-making, helping ensure that future responses to displacement are well-planned, inclusive, and sustainable.

    MEANS OF IMPLEMENTATION

    5.1. Institutional arrangement

    The primary institutions responsible for NDC implementation and achievement include:

    The Ministry of Ecology and Natural Resources of the Republic of Kazakhstan (MENR) is the primary body for coordination and implementation of the provisions of the Convention and the Paris Agreement. It is a lead agency for climate change, environmental protection, coordination of the UNFCCC and Paris Agreement implementation.

    MENR is the working body of the Council on the Transition to the Green Economy, which is a coordinating body for sustainable development issues like climate change. The Council focuses on monitoring and assessing the implementation of the Concept for the Transition to a Green Economy, and provides recommendations based on these assessments.

    Furthermore, MENR is the leading institution in the Environmental Code and a key partner in ETS regulation. MENR is also helping to develop the National Adaptation Plan, setting forth adaptation strategies and resiliency planning.

    The Department of Climate Policy, within MENR, formulates and coordinates state policy on climate and ozone layer protection. This department also implements provisions of the UNFCCC and other international agreements and oversees state policy on international cooperation.

    The Forestry and Wildlife Committee implements and supervises functions related to forestry, wildlife protection, and use of specially protected natural areas.

    JSC Zhasyl Damu, a subsidiary of MENR, prepares the National Greenhouse Gas Inventory Report annually for submission under the UNFCCC.

    Data collection is based on information from the Bureau of National Statistics and official submissions from sectoral ministries. The organization also serves as the operator of the national Emissions Trading System, ensuring its administration, monitoring, and the development of carbon regulation instruments. In addition, Zhasyl Damu contributes to improving national climate policy and legislation and conducts analytical studies to support evidence-based decision-making on greenhouse gas emission regulation.

    The Agency for Strategic Planning and Reforms, which houses the Bureau of National Statistics and directly reports to the President, analyses and disseminates statistical data for the national GHG inventory, as well as data on other indicators to track progress towards the NDC targets.

    The Ministry of Energy is the body overseeing and implementing state energy policy and planning in the Republic of Kazakhstan. Among other things, it regulates production of petroleum products, gas and electricity supply, nuclear energy and is central in developing renewable energy sources.

    Specific targets for renewable energy sources are also set forth in the Strategic Plan of the Ministry of Energy of Kazakhstan for 2023- 2027 , which establishes the goal of generating 50% of all energy by 2050 through expanding renewable energy capacity and storage, developing nuclear energy projects, and investing in alternative energy sources.

    The Republic of Kazakhstan has a variety of interagency working groups, such as those under MENR, that coordinate across ministries to align on high-level climate and environmental policies. Youth participation is being institutionalized through forums, working groups, and representation in public councils. At least 30% youth representation is ensured in decision-making bodies under the Ministry of Ecology and at the local level.

    The Action Plan for the Development of the Electric Power Industry until 2035 is focused on increasing the share of renewable energy sources in electricity generation, including wind power with energy storage, solar power plants, and hydroelectricity.

    The ETS Regulatory Body, which as previously mentioned is operated by MENR, provides the legal and technical frameworks for the ETS, working with partners like the PMI Trust Fund through a World Bank Initiative to strengthen the ETS.

    Another critical point of cross-sectoral coordination is planning documents like the National Development Plan, which serve to integrate strategy across economy, energy transition, and development goals, as well as the strategic actions plans and their respective sectoral road maps, which link investments and regulations to targets and strategies.

    5.2. Monitoring, reporting and verification

    To accelerate NDC implementation, Kazakhstan aims to improve data transparency, information, and analysis related to monitoring and evaluation of climate data, as well as financial, technological, and capacity building support.

    A National Inventory Report has been prepared annually, tracking emissions and removals since 1990.

    The system is complemented by State Carbon and Waste Inventories, a State Register of Carbon Units, and a Pollutant Release and Transfer Register, which provide data for assessing the effectiveness of national climate policy.

    The core of the national MRV system is the National Greenhouse Gas Inventory, maintained by the Ministry of Ecology and Natural Resources.

    Remote sensing (RS) and geographic information system (GIS) data play a key role within the NIR system. The use of RS and GIS as tools for NIR ensures the generation of reliable and objective data.

    Their comprehensive integration into monitoring and reporting processes enables the generation of long-term and comparable environmental data, and their consistent application creates the basis for transparent and verifiable national reporting, particularly for the National Greenhouse Gas Inventory.

    In this regard, unified sets of quality standards (quality assurance/quality control) and data validation protocols will be developed to ensure the consistency, reliability, and comparability of information used in national monitoring, planning, and reporting systems.

    These standards will form the basis for integrating climate data across various agencies and ensure unified approaches to the development of climate change reporting.

    Progress on climate targets is also tracked through national statistical and governance platforms. The Bureau of National Statistics and the e-Government platform supply open data to government and the public for oversight of government agencies’ progress on strategic development plans. The Council on the Transition to a Green Economy, together with the Ministry of Energy and the Ministry of Ecology and Natural Resources, reviews the impacts of mitigation and adaptation measures.

    Technology transfer is imperative to advancing mitigation, adaptation, and a larger shift toward a low-carbon economy.

    5.3 Support needs to enhance NDC implementation capacities

    At the same time, capacity building is essential for implementing mitigation and adaptation measures as mentioned in the NDC and establishing the requisite technical expertise for technology transfer to be effective.

    Advanced technologies can catalyze renewable energy deployment, carbon capture and storage, smart grids, water-saving technologies, and deep electrification and weatherization.

    Investing in capacity building, through actions like promoting international best practices and providing training and educational seminars to disseminate information around “green” transition and low- carbon technologies, can help develop critical local expertise, improve policy implementation, and implement projects in key sectors such as energy, industry, and agriculture.

    Kazakhstan has demonstrated strong domestic commitment, financing the bulk of its mitigation and adaptation measures from its own budget and state-owned enterprises.

    Yet, achieving the ambition of NDC 3.0 and the 2060 Carbon-Neutrality Strategy will require significantly more external support.

    Key priorities for international assistance include:

    • Financing the transition - concessional lending, equity, grants and blended-finance facilities to modernize ageing infrastructure, scale up renewables and energy efficiency, expand climate-resilient water, transport and urban systems, and crowd-in private investment.

    • Technology transfer and capacity-building - access to advanced low-carbon technologies (hydrogen, CCUS, grid integration, drought- resilient farming, efficient heating/cooling) and training of national experts to deploy and maintain them.

    • Just transition support - planning and implementing measures to reskill workers, diversify coal-dependent regional economies, and provide targeted social-protection schemes.

    • MRV and data systems - building a unified national system for GHG inventories (built on high-tier methods and approaches), mitigation and adaptation tracking, including modernized reporting tools and interoperable data platforms.

  • Strengthening Kazakhstan’s emissions trading system as a core market-based instrument

    Kazakhstan operates the region’s only Emissions Trading System , which currently covers the power and industrial sectors. Broadening ETS coverage and increasing carbon pricing can generate new revenue while driving emissions reductions.

    The Environmental Code (2021, amended 2025) provides a modern legal basis for the ETS. It defines the role of the Ministry of Ecology and Natural Resources as the competent authority, for delivering cost-effective mitigation.

    Expansion and Linkages

    Kazakhstan is considering the gradual expansion of carbon pricing to additional sectors such as transport, waste, and agriculture . The potential introduction of a carbon-tax mechanism implemented through the existing ETS infrastructure is also being evaluated to cover emission sources not yet included in the trading system.

    With continued reform, stronger integration with Article 6 mechanisms, and enhanced market confidence, it can mobilise domestic and international finance for low-carbon transition while ensuring that Kazakhstan’s NDC targets are met in an efficient and transparent manner.

    These developments will ensure consistent carbon-price signals, administrative efficiency, and compatibility between market-based and fiscal instruments.

    A Carbon Fund will be established as a catalytic financing platform pooling revenues from ETS auctions, Article 6 transactions, and domestic and international contributions.

    Carbon Fund

    The Fund will:

    Co-finance mitigation and adaptation projects, leveraging public resources to mobilize private capital and concessional finance;

    Support investment in low-carbon technologies, infrastructure modernization, and innovation in priority sectors;

    Align domestic investment flows with international climate-finance programs, strengthening the link between policy and finance.

    Kazakhstan will undertake a comprehensive reform of its national offset framework by the end of 2026 to strengthen integrity, transparency, and compatibility with international carbon markets.

    Offset Reform and Integration with Carbon Markets

    The revised system will:

    Align additionality criteria, baseline methodologies, and MRV procedures with international best practices while reflecting national circumstances and sectoral priorities;

    Reconsider eligibility to mitigation activities such as renewables (large- scale and distributed), energy efficiency for industrial modernization and buildings, methane reduction, and land-use management, but not limited to;

    Enable the generation of high-quality offset units that can be

    used for domestic compliance, voluntary markets, or transferred internationally under Article 6 cooperative approaches;

    Evaluate the interaction between the ETS and Article 6 cooperative approaches to ensure legal and methodological consistency, compatibility of registries and MRV systems, alignment of price signals and investment flows, and avoidance of double counting, defining how international carbon transactions can complement domestic carbon pricing while safeguarding NDC integrity and supporting national mitigation priorities.

    Evaluate the introduction of transition-crediting mechanisms to ensure alignment with national mitigation efforts, ETS and Article 6 rules, while rewarding early emission reductions from industrial modernization, fleet renewal, and technology transfer, and supporting gradual sectoral integration into carbon markets.

    Transition Plans

    Major emitting enterprises will be required to prepare Transition Plans outlining their greenhouse-gas inventories, production forecasts, technological processes, and decarbonization pathways.

    Plans:

    Improve transparency and accountability at the enterprise level;

    Align corporate strategies with national emission- reduction objectives;

    Facilitate access to climate finance and guide the prioritization of Carbon Fund investments toward sectors with the highest mitigation potential and readiness for implementation.

    Country Platform

    To integrate these instruments into a unified framework, Kazakhstan will establish a Country Platform for Climate Finance Mobilization, a coordination mechanism linking government agencies, private- sector actors, and international partners.

    The Platform will:

    Connect the ETS, Article 6 framework, Carbon Fund, and Transition Plans into a single system for project preparation, financing, and monitoring;

    Develop a pipeline of bankable projects and facilitate blended-finance operations;

    Align domestic financial markets with international green- finance standards, including green-bond frameworks, disclosure, and taxonomy alignment.

    Enabling Market Confidence and Linking with Article 6

    The reformed ETS is designed not only as a domestic mitigation instrument but also as a gateway for international cooperation under Article 6 of the Paris Agreement.

    The legal framework allows:

    • The issuance and transfer of internationally transferred mitigation outcomes (ITMOs),

    • Corresponding adjustments to avoid double counting,

    • Future linkage of the national carbon market with regional or global markets.

      In addition, Kazakhstan will utilize Article 6 cooperative approaches to pilot emerging low-carbon technologies, such as hydrogen, carbon capture, utilization and storage (CCUS), Power-to-X, and long-duration energy storage, but limited by them. Through international partnerships, climate finance programs and results-based finance, Article 6 will be used to channel carbon revenues toward demonstration projects that accelerate technology deployment, foster innovation, and build domestic capacity for large-scale implementation in hard-to-abate sectors

  • Article 6 cooperative approaches

    Kazakhstan recognizes the role of cooperative approaches under Article 6 as a key instrument to help achieve its NDC targets while catalyzing investment and innovation for sustainable development.

    Amendments to the Environmental Code (2025) provide a dedicated legal basis for implementing both Article 6.2 (bilateral/club-based cooperation) and Article 6.4 (UNFCCC-supervised mechanism), as well as for establishing a voluntary carbon market (VCM).

    Key elements include:

    • Legal definitions of ITMOs, corresponding adjustments (CA), “baseline scenario,” “additionality,” and provisions for voluntary markets.

    • Clear mandates for the Ministry of Ecology and Natural Resources (MENR) as the national authority and for Jasyl Damu JSC as the operator of the carbon market and national registry.

    • Rules for accounting, registration, issuance, and transfer of units, including a buffer account to hedge against natural risks (fires, drought) and safeguard environmental integrity.

    • Alignment with CMA decisions 2/CMA.3 and 3/CMA.3 (Glasgow 2021) to ensure transparency, prevent double counting, and uphold sustainable-development safeguards.

    An Article 6 Technical Working Group under MENR, comprising relevant ministries (energy, finance, economy, industry, agriculture, foreign affairs), sub-national authorities, and private-sector stakeholders, which reviews project methodologies, approves baseline parameters, and advises on authorizations. This governance architecture enables Kazakhstan to participate credibly in international carbon markets and to integrate Article 6 into national mitigation planning.

    The country has already:

    • Signed bilateral cooperation agreements with Japan and the Republic of Korea, focused on developing rules, guidelines and institutional arrangements for ITMO transfers.

    • Committed to its NDC to explore ETS-linkage and other cooperative mechanisms that can help mobilize carbon finance and accelerate transition in key sectors.

    The Republic of Kazakhstan recognizes that participation in the Article 6 mechanisms of the Paris Agreement should be strategic and phased in order to avoid “overselling” the emission reductions needed to achieve its own NDC targets, while maximizing the socio-economic benefits of incoming carbon finance.

    5.5.1. Key participation requirements for 6 projects

    To maintain environmental integrity and avoid double counting, all cooperative-approach activities must, inter alia:

    • Demonstrate additionality beyond policies and actions already included in Kazakhstan’s NDC or ETS baseline.

    • Apply approved international methodologies and national baseline parameters validated by the Article 6 Technical Working Group.

    • Ensure robust MRV systems integrated with the national GHG inventory and registry.

    • Contribute to sustainable-development co-benefits (e.g., job creation, technology transfer, gender and youth inclusion).

    • Implement social and environmental safeguards and respect community and indigenous rights where applicable.

    • Comply with national rules for corresponding adjustments to maintain accuracy of Kazakhstan’s carbon budget.

    Activities that are included in the unconditional target of Kazakhstan’s NDC or that are already included in the ETS without clearly demonstrated additionality are not eligible for participation in Article 6 mechanisms.

    6.4. International climate finance

    In 2023 alone, Kazakhstan’s environmental and climate-related spending exceeded USD 1.3 billion, complemented by significant capital investments in renewable energy, resilient water infrastructure, disaster risk management and other priority sectors.

    Kazakhstan has made substantial domestic investments in climate action, financing the majority of mitigation and adaptation measures from national and local budgets, state-owned enterprises (SOEs), and households.

    In contrast, international climate finance mobilized by Kazakhstan remains limited and focused mainly on technical assistance and capacity-building rather than large-scale concessional investment or technology transfer.

    Over the past 15 years, Kazakhstan has received less than USD 1 billion in direct climate-related support from dedicated international funds, much of it through multilateral development banks (MDBs) as loans rather than grants.

    Kazakhstan’s experience demonstrates that while international finance plays an important enabling role, particularly by supporting technical cooperation, policy reforms, and capacity-building, the scale and especially concessionality of external support remains insufficient to match the country’s ambition and vulnerability to climate impacts.

    To meet the investment needs of NDC 3.0 and achieve a resilient, low- carbon transition, Kazakhstan emphasizes the need for larger volumes and more diverse international financing:

    • predictable and longer-term external finance to complement domestic investment;

    • stronger grant-based support and concessional lending for adaptation, nature-based solutions, and just transition measures;

    • equity investments, risk-sharing schemes, subordinated and mezzanine loans;

    • technology transfer and partnerships for advanced mitigation options such as hydrogen, CCUS, grid integration and climate-smart agriculture.

    Kazakhstan calls on the international community and its partners to better align climate finance flows with national needs and to ensure that countries with high climate ambition but limited access to concessional funding receive the support necessary to deliver on their NDC commitments.

    Mitigation co-benefits resulting from adaptation actions and/or economic diversification plans, including description of specific projects, measures and initiatives of adaptation actions and/or economic diversification plans.

    Kazakhstan's priority is to promote measures that maximize co-benefits and minimize the risks of increased emissions or unsustainable resource use. In particular:

    • - Modernizing irrigation and agricultural technologies increase agricultural resilience to droughts while reducing energy consumption and emissions through the implementation of water-saving technologies and soil carbon sequestration.

    • Modernizing power grids and improving their efficiency increases the resilience of the power system to extreme heat and reduces energy losses, thereby reducing indirect emissions from generation.

    • Reforestation and afforestation strengthen the adaptive capacity of ecosystems (water retention, soil protection, biodiversity) while simultaneously promoting carbon sequestration.

    • Cooling measures for buildings to protect public health, although increasing energy demand, will be implemented using energy-efficient standards, passive architectural solutions, environmentally friendly refrigerants, and demand-side management systems to prevent emissions growth.

    The Republic of Kazakhstan will continue to integrate climate action into a unified strategic framework, combining adaptation and mitigation to enhance overall climate resilience, reduce emissions, and protect the health and well-being of the population, in line with SDGs 2, 3, 7, 9, 13, and 15.

    4. Planning processes

    (a)

    Information on the planning processes that the Party undertook to prepare its nationally determined contribution

    The development of NDC 3.0 was an inclusive national process led by the Ministry of Ecology and Natural Resources. The Working Group on NDC 3.0 was chaired by the First Deputy Prime Minister. The Working group included 14 sectoral ministries and agencies responsible for energy, industry, agriculture, transport, finance, economy, and other sectors. Each institution designated focal points to provide data, validate modelling assumptions, and review policy measures.

    The Working group followed a structured workplan endorsed by the Vice-Prime Minister to ensure consistency with national development priorities and alignment with three other national strategic initiatives that were underway in parallel: the preparation of the Roadmap for the Strategy on Achieving Carbon Neutrality by 2060, the drafting of the National Adaptation Plan, and the update of the Concept for Transition to a Green Economy, which is overseen by the President of Kazakhstan.

    Broad stakeholder consultations were conducted between 2023 and 2025, including technical roundtables with energy, industry, transport, AFOLU, waste, and adaptation sectors, as well as regional workshops and youth dialogues. Analytical work, including sectoral modelling and cost estimations, was undertaken by national and international experts. International partners such as UNDP, FAO, GIZ, NDC Partnership, UNICEF, UNITAR, Stockholm Environment Institute provided technical inputs and capacity-building support throughout the process.
