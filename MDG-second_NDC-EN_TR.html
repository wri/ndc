1. CONTEXT

The Republic of Madagascar is located to the east of the African coast. The territory includes the Big Island which stretches from north to south over a geographical distance of more than 1,500 km, between 12 and 25° south latitude, almost in an intertropical environment, and occupies an area of 587,041 km2. Madagascar also has maritime territories with islets, atolls and archipelagos that are submersible due to the increase in sea level.

The climate of Madagascar is subdivided into two seasons: a hot and rainy season, from November to April, and a cool and dry season between May and October. The hot season is characterized by the formation of cyclonic disturbances in the southwest Indian Ocean basin, affecting the country on average three to five times per year. The average annual temperature on the Big Island is 24 to 27°C. The average annual precipitation decreases from North to South, from 1,500 mm to minus 0.400 mm per year.

Regarding climate change, the rise in temperatures is reflected in an increase of 0.27°C in the national average every 10 years. The change in the rainfall regime is expressed by the lengthening of dry seasons, the intensification of torrential rains and an 8% reduction in precipitation since 1990. Between 1990 and 2020, Madagascar recorded 70 major climatic disasters including 64 cyclonic disturbances and six episodes of severe drought. Sea level rise reached 0.6 cm per year between 1994 and 2008. By 2050, the following changes are predicted to occur with high probability: decreases in precipitation of up to -8%; reduction in the quantity of water available by 25% in the North and East and by 40% in the South-West; increase in temperatures from +1.2°C to +2.1°C; and sea level rise of up to 43 cm (2080).

In 2020, Madagascar has 27.2 million inhabitants. The population growth rate is 3%. The population is very young: more than 85% of residents are under 45 years old and around 28% are young children (under 10 years old). The rural-urban disparity is very marked in many aspects of socio-economic development, including the inequality of population distribution and the distribution of wealth in general. Madagascar is a less advanced country, not very industrialized and with sparse infrastructure. The national economy depends heavily on primary sectors. More than 83% of the population makes a living from agriculture. Rice cultivation dominates, employing 80% of the agricultural population and constitutes the most cultivated food commodity. Cash crops are 90% exported, represent 30% of exports, and constitute the most important sources of added value in the national economy. Annual agricultural growths (1.0% during the last decade) are often wiped out by climatic fluctuations and other endogenous factors. Fisheries resources represent approximately 6% of GDP and 6% of exports. The exploitation of fishing potential is not optimized and only employs 5% of the population. The exclusive economic zone and the vast coastal and mangrove areas favorable to aquaculture are almost unexploited, and current activities are suffering the harmful effects of the destruction of mangroves and coral reefs and global warming. Tourism, 15% of GDP in 2020, depends mainly on natural ecosystems and biodiversity, and tourism infrastructure is sensitive to cyclonic hazards.

For Madagascar, access to sustainable development and the fight against poverty constitute the pillars of the fight against the harmful effects of global warming. In 2020, multidimensional poverty reached 74% and the prevalence of malnutrition 48% of the population. The essential causes of malnutrition are poverty, lack of education, climatic hazards, traditional agricultural practices and insufficient resources for irrigation infrastructure. From 2015, the food insecurity alert zones are focused in the Great South, where severe food insecurity affects 1.31 million people in December 2021 and 500,000 people have urgent nutritional needs. . Over the last 10 years, Madagascar has recorded an increasing number of deaths due to climate-sensitive diseases. Deforestation and the destruction of natural ecosystems are among the greatest drivers of climate vulnerability, through the loss of ecosystem services. In 2020, the total forest cover was estimated at 12.4 million ha, including 5.6 million ha of evergreen humid forests and 6.7 million ha of dry forests, thickets, mangroves and reforestation forests. The average annual deforestation rate is 1.5% in 2019. Land degradation affects 12 of Madagascar's 23 regions, with an area of 70,000 km2 requiring immediate action in 2024.

Forest loss and land use change are the most important causes of the decline in national capacities for absorbing greenhouse gases. Between 2010-2020, these are the main sources of greenhouse gases, representing more than 80% of national emissions. Agriculture is the second largest emitter of greenhouse gases, contributing to 16% of national emissions. Subsistence agriculture (traditional rice cultivation, corn, cassava, extensive cattle breeding) is rapidly destroying forest and wetland carbon stocks.

In 2010, Energy was responsible for 2% of national emissions. The Residential subcategory (including cooking energy: more than 90% of the population uses wood energy as fuel) remains the largest user of energy. It is followed by the Energy Industries. The consumption of fossil fuels for transport (road and rail) is estimated at half of the total volume imported, or approximately 500,000 m3. The country's hydrocarbon supply is exclusively dependent on imports, and the national energy supply chain is extremely vulnerable to climatic hazards. The energy industries and transport are important factors in socio-economic development, but the related infrastructures are regularly impacted by cyclonic disturbances and lack maintenance. “Industrial Processes and Product Use” and “Waste” are not together contribute about 0.4% of national emissions in 2010. Emissions from the Waste sector tend to increase with rapid population growth. For the Industrial Processes and Product Use sector, the country's raw material potential calls for caution.

Trends in national emissions and removals call for more rigorous mitigation actions. Madagascar considers that these needs to contribute more to global efforts to reduce emissions should benefit the sustainable development and environmental integrity of the country. These contributions require political and strategic frameworks to create favorable and incentivizing environments, strengthening the participation of all actors at all levels.

Madagascar is making efforts to achieve sustainable development. These efforts are based on the modernization of existing production sectors and industrialization. Certain daily needs, for which the country has significant potential (textiles and clothing, construction materials, medicines, hydrocarbons, etc.), are almost exclusively imported. The situations observed within each sector (and the projected scenarios) of socio-economic development call for strengthening capacities for adaptation and reduction of climate vulnerabilities.

The CDN2 takes into account existing strategic documents such as (i) the revised National Policy to Combat Climate Change (PNLCCr, 2021) which serves as a reference for all actions to combat climate change in Madagascar; (ii) the National Appropriate Mitigation Action (ANAA, 2003) which aims for reductions in greenhouse gas emissions in a context of sustainable development; (iii) the country also has a National Adaptation Plan (PNA, 2021) which will serve as a strategic framework for priority adaptation actions in the medium and long term. This document is also linked to the General State Policy (PGE) including the Madagascar Emergency Plan (South) and the Madagascar Emergence Plan (PEM) laws, policies, strategies, existing national and sectoral plans.

Thus, the CDN2 is positioned as a more ambitious strategic planning document to combat climate change for the next five years to which all actions at the national level should refer. A list of the main reference instruments is given in Section 5 (Institutional Arrangement). The Government of Madagascar has entrusted the development of CDN2 to the Ministry of Environment and Sustainable Development (MEDD). This process involved the departments and institutions responsible for implementing sectoral programs to combat climate change, as well as decentralized local authorities, technical and financial partners, the private sector and civil society organizations.

Progression from the previous Nationally Determined Contribution (Article 4(3) of the Paris Agreement), gradual shift towards economy-wide emission reduction targets, taking into account different national situations

Sources and greenhouse gases and their impacts

The CDN1 only considered the following sectors: Agriculture, Energy LULUCF and Waste. CDN2 included the Industrial Processes and Product Use sector. Which raised the ambitions compared to the sources.

Ambitions to reduce emissions and strengthen absorption capacities

In 2030, the CDN2 targets a 28% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions, or 48,403 Gg eq. CO2. In addition to this reduction in emissions, CDN2 aims to strengthen its greenhouse gas absorption capacity by around 20%, or -37,809 Gg eq. CO2 from additional sequestration.

In 2030, the CDN1 targeted a 14% reduction in direct greenhouse gas emissions, equivalent to 29,990 Gg eq. CO2 and additional sequestration of -61,000 Gg eq. CO2, i.e. 32% reinforcement of carbon sinks. The mitigation ambitions presented in CDN2 appear to have regressed compared to those of CDN1; but they are realistic and achievable, and reflect the realities of current and projected sectoral policies and socio-economic contexts. Concerning the LULUCF sector, the mitigation potentials of mitigation actions were not fully included in CDN2 since only 25-30% of the sector's mitigation results will be expressed by 2030.

Methodological and metric approaches used for estimating and accounting for greenhouse gas emissions and absorption

The national greenhouse gas inventories carried out in Madagascar (CNI, DCN, TCN and CDN1) serve as the basis for the development of the CDN2. These inventories use the Revised 1996 IPCC Guidelines, supported by the IPCC Recommendations on Good Practice and Uncertainty Management for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories (GPG 2000) and the Good Practice Recommendations for the LULUCF sector (GPG 2003). All greenhouse gas inventories at sector level are Tier 1.

Madagascar is currently developing its first Biennial Updated Report and its Fourth National Communication. The results of the inventories corresponding to these national reports will make it possible to update greenhouse gas estimates and emissions. They will feed into the update of the CDN from the year 2025.

The CDN2 includes sectoral and regulatory policy development activities which should enable the country to gradually expand its climate actions, in parallel with its sustainable development objectives. Furthermore, the implementation of many climate actions are cross-sectoral and are actions to scale up initiatives already started before 2020.

The operationalization of national and sectoral MRV systems will make it possible to collect data which will allow the country to gradually move towards a political reflex to reduce emissions on an economy-wide scale. To this end, as stipulated by Article 4, paragraph 5, of the Paris Agreement, as a developing country and adaptation is a priority, the Government of Madagascar intends to benefit from the support of other Parties to the 'Paris Agreement for the achievement of the objectives contained in its CDN2

2. ATTENUATION

Madagascar is still classified as a carbon sink country, hence the importance of this mitigation component. It should nevertheless be noted that adaptation is the priority aspect for Madagascar given that the country is among the most vulnerable to climate change in the world.

2.1 QUANTIFIED NATIONAL CONTRIBUTIONS

In 2030, Madagascar aims to reduce its emissions to 48,403 Gg eq. CO2, a reduction of 28% compared to the normal business scenario. To this reduction in emissions will be added the strengthening of greenhouse gas sinks of the order of 20%, representing avoided emissions and the strengthening of carbon sinks of the order of 37,809 Gg eq. CO2 from the Land Use and Land Use Change and Forests (LULUCF) sector.

Without strengthening mitigation actions, Madagascar will exit its carbon sink status just after the year 2025 when the country will display a balance of -1,734 Gg eq. CO2 against its status as an absorbing sink -63,072 Gg eq. CO2 in 2010 and -23,609 Gg eq. CO2 in 2020. By 2030, by omitting mitigation policies and measures in sectoral policies, Madagascar will display a net balance emitting 22,578 Gg eq. CO2.

3. ADAPTATION

3.1 ADAPTATION OBJECTS

Madagascar aims to strengthen its national adaptation capacities and reduce climate risks, more particularly taking into account the following two points:

  • All investments will take into account the reduction of climate risks and vulnerabilities

  • Human, material and institutional infrastructures are strengthened, at all levels, to face climate risks.

The adaptation objectives will be achieved through the application of cross-cutting and sectoral measures. In addition, the climate actions in this section will also aim to “strengthen the resilience to climate change of the 32.3 million Malagasy people in 2030”.

3.2 ADAPTATION MEASURES

3.2.1 TRANSVERSE MEASUREMENTS

The cross-cutting measures concern several sectors and must be applied to achieve adaptation objectives:

  • Effectively apply sectoral standards and/or rules already established or initiated, including infrastructure standards resistant to climatic hazards;

  • Strengthen the improvement of cyclone early warning systems and scale up multi-risk early warning systems by integrating phytosanitary surveillance, agricultural warnings, drought alerts and food and nutritional surveillance;

  • Monitor climate information in real time;

  • Develop and update territorial planning and management taking into account the sustainable management of natural resources and adaptation to climate change;

  • Execute the implementation of territorial planning and management taking into account their multi-sectoral nature;

  • Strengthen and apply research results for all sectors affected by adaptation measures:

  • Provision of quantitative data and provision of sustainable solutions against saline infiltration and salinization;

  • Promotion of health-climate research;

  • Deployment of monitoring and research activities for the agriculture-livestock sector; improvement of knowledge of (1) the impacts of increasing temperature on food commodities, (2) climatic hazards on factors affecting the sector: animal pests, invasive species, emerging diseases, etc. ; determination of the extent and impacts of water salinization and saline infiltration on crop plots; promotion of scientific research as well as its application to the development of livestock breeding in Madagascar;

  • Research and innovations on agrobiodiversity.

3.2.2 AGRICULTURE - LIVESTOCK

The national economy depends on the agriculture sector, it represents 24.1% of GDP. However, this sector is among the sectors most vulnerable to climate change. The measures taken with regard to Agriculture cited below are aligned with the (i) PEM in its commitment no. 1 and no. 2 aimed at food self-sufficiency, (ii) the National Policy to combat Change Revised Climate Change (PNLCCr) in its strategic axis 2 on strengthening adaptation and reducing vulnerability, as well as with (iii) programs n°1 and 2 of the National Adaptation Plan which targets the resilience of agrosystems and 'adaptation of agricultural and livestock practices to climatic conditions

  • Strengthen strategic benchmarks, management plans and knowledge bases on vulnerability and responses to climate change (cross-sectoral prospects);

  • Facilitate access to means of implementation, particularly in relation to agricultural credit and agricultural insurance;

  • Promotion of resilient practices on the livestock system, on the modernization of agricultural production systems and on the use of Agricultural Emergence Zones;

  • Implementation of standards (agricultural infrastructure) and implementation plans;

  • Capacity building on innovative sustainable agriculture techniques and strengthening the resilience of livestock (including breed improvement/conservation);

  • Development of monitoring and early warning systems specifically on salinization and on pests and diseases.

3.2.3 WATER RESOURCES

Water availability risks decreasing with global warming. Integrated water resources management and water resources planning are essential to ensure the sustainability of water resources. The measures taken within the framework of this document are aligned with (i) the PEM in its commitment no. 9 on Energy and water for all, (ii) the PNLCC revised in its strategic axis 2 on strengthening actions adaptation, and on the (iii) PNA through structuring program no. 4 “improving access to drinking water in urban and rural areas”

The measures to be implemented for water resources are as follows:

  • Strengthen strategic benchmarks, management plans and knowledge bases on vulnerability and responses to climate change;

  • Facilitate access to means of implementation by investing in water control (production and food);

  • Strengthen the governance of infrastructure and management systems and support the effectiveness of integrated water resources management;

  • Promotion of resilient practices and sustainable resource development;

  • Implementation of standards and implementation plans (sanitation and drinking water infrastructure);

  • Capacity building (institutional and technical).

  • Update databases and strengthen monitoring and early warning systems

3.2.4 FORESTS AND BIODIVERSITY

Land degradation and loss of biodiversity linked to the harmful effects of climate change constitute a threat to Madagascar. The measures taken in CDN2 for the forestry and biodiversity sector aim on the one hand to reduce the vulnerability of ecosystems and on the other hand to find synergy between conservation and mitigation of climate change. These measures align with (i) the PEM through commitment no. 13 on the sustainable management and conservation of our natural resources, (ii) the revised PNLCC through strategic axis 1 on strengthening the contributions of mitigation in line with sustainable development, and axis 2 on strengthening adaptation actions, (iii) the National Adaptation Plan through program 6 on the acceleration of reforestation through the operationalization of the REDD+ mechanism and development of ecosystem services, and program 7 on improving the conservation of natural forests and management of protected areas

The measures to be taken for this sector are presented below:

  • Strengthen strategic benchmarks on the large-scale development of sectors on forest phylogenetic resources, as well as management schemes and knowledge bases on vulnerability and responses to climate change;

  • Facilitate the means of implementation, strengthen favorable conditions for investments in fair value chains as well as private investments in forest management actions;

  • Strengthen infrastructure governance, management systems, in-situ and ex situ conservation of threatened species

  • Promotion of resilient practices, sustainable development of resources, strengthening the resilience of natural eco-systems and ecosystem services through reforestation and restoration;

  • Establish an ecosystem-based adaptation process;

  • Strengthening databases and updating data on harmful and invasive species and the geographical distribution of biota by identifying priority areas for conservation;

  • Development of monitoring and early warning systems

3.2.5 PUBLIC HEALTH

Illnesses linked to rising temperatures are increasing in Madagascar. The measures taken in this document for the health sector aim to respond to the main needs of the health sector with regard to the need for increased investments to strengthen health infrastructure, and the reduction of vulnerability to climate-sensitive diseases. These measures align with the (i) PEM through commitment no. 5 stipulating that health is an inalienable right for every citizen, (ii) the revised PNLCC through strategic axis no. 2 on strengthening adaptation, and (iii) PNA through program no. 5 on strengthening early warning systems for the resilience of the health sector in the face of climate change.

The measures specified for this sector are listed below:

  • Strengthen strategic benchmarks, management plans and knowledge bases on vulnerability and responses to climate change;

  • Promotion of resilient practices, intensification of interventions in health responses to climate-sensitive diseases, and standardization of health infrastructure infrastructure, medical waste management infrastructure;

  • Strengthen the capacities of the health system and stakeholders in terms of adaptation to climate change and integrate the theme of Health and Climate Change into the research program of institutions;

  • Update databases and develop monitoring-evaluation mechanisms for health climate;

  • Development of monitoring and early warning systems.

3.2.6 COASTAL ZONES

Rising sea levels and receding coastlines due to climate change are already affecting the population living in coastal areas. The measures included in this CDN2 aim for integrated management of coastal zones, reduction of degradation of coastal environments, and sustainable development. These measures align with (i) the PEM through commitment no. 13 on the sustainable management and conservation of our natural resources, and commitment no. 11 on the tourism industry, the revised PNLCC through Axis No. 2 on strengthening resilience, and the PNA through program 8 on the protection of coastal infrastructure and economic activities.

The adaptation measures opted for this sector are as follows:

  • Strengthen strategic benchmarks, management plans and knowledge bases on vulnerability and responses to climate change;

  • Promotion of resilient practices, sustainable development of resources, and strengthening of the protection of coastal and coastal infrastructure and economic activities;

  • Strengthening technical, institutional and operational capacities for integrated coastal zone management;

  • Update databases and information systems on climatic hazards and impacts on urban areas, agricultural and fishery production areas

  • Development of monitoring and early warning systems, strengthening restoration plans, habitat connectivity of coastal, coastal and marine ecosystems;

  • Triplement of the surface area of marine protected areas, scaling up of conservation actions and sustainable participatory management of coastal and coastal resources, strengthening of natural protections and reduction of the vulnerability of coastal, marine and coastal areas concerned by coastal erosion and coastal retreat

3.2.7 TERRITORY DEVELOPMENT

As part of the implementation of the “Modernization of Madagascar”, measures to adapt cities to climate change are planned. The establishment of costs was done according to the budgets established by following the budget lines of the State Secretariat in charge of New Cities and Housing or in relation to the prospective studies which have already been carried out.

The actions to be taken will relate to the integration of the establishment of the new city into national efforts to adapt to climate change as a resilient zero-carbon city. It is in fact about planning territorial development in accordance with adaptation to climatic hazards and releasing the least amount of carbon (Zero Carbon).

3.2.8 RISKS AND DISASTERS

The measures to take into account in terms of risks and disasters are as follows:

  • Strengthen strategic benchmarks, management plans and knowledge bases on vulnerability and responses to climate change;

  • Facilitate access to means of implementation;

  • Strengthen the governance of infrastructure and management systems;

  • Promotion of resilient practices and sustainable resource development;

  • Implementation of standards and implementation plans.

To conclude, these adaptation measures will reduce vulnerability and strengthen the country's resilience. Indeed, the implementation of the PNA will contribute to achieving the objectives of the CDN. These measures should be integrated and prioritized in sectoral and national programming. Thus, coordination of financial mobilization is necessary and essential for implementation.

4. MEANS OF IMPLEMENTATION

Madagascar, as a country very vulnerable to climatic hazards, allocates a substantial part of its annual budget to infrastructure and social service systems in order to help resolve the harmful effects of climate change. Since the country submitted its National Appropriate Mitigation Action document in 2011, actions to reduce GHG emissions and strengthen carbon sinks are increasingly considered as an integral part of the country's sustainable development actions.

4.1 GAPS AND BARRIERS

Madagascar has no historical responsibility for greenhouse gas emissions and their impacts. The country is currently on the brink of losing its status as a greenhouse gas sink; but at the same time, Madagascar is a country with a weak economic and social situation. From the above, most of the means of implementing CDN2 will depend on the availability of external financial resources, capacity building actions and transfer of innovative technologies from developed countries or developing countries able to provide support in the fight against climate change.

Barriers to the implementation of CDN2 adaptation and mitigation activities in the Republic of Madagascar include, among others:

  • Weak capacity (knowledge, techniques, technology, resource mobilization);

  • Insufficient capacity for the development and mobilization of climate-smart technologies;

  • The weak consideration of gender, climate empowerment and the weak participation of all stakeholders;

  • Lack of resources for the development of framework documents, benchmarks and standards.

4.2 CAPACITY BUILDING

Human resources potential exists within the various structures involved in the implementation of actions to combat climate change. These executives need an upgrade to properly carry out their roles. Capacity building essentially concerns the mastery of new tools and methods in terms of programming, leadership, monitoring-evaluation, mobilization of resources as well as the provision of materials and work equipment. All these capacity building actions are recommended by the Revised National Policy to Combat Climate Change (PNCCR). Priority capacity building actions concern:

  • Periodic assessment of national needs in terms of capacity building;

  • Strengthening the national coordination structure for greenhouse gas inventory, planning and programming of mitigation projects;

  • Accounting for emissions and emissions reductions, through the development of country-specific emissions factors and the improvement of the national GHG inventory;

  • Identifying and updating the needs corresponding to the reduction of climate risks and vulnerabilities and the strengthening of national mitigation potential;

  • Involvement of national, subnational and local actors in monitoring climate parameters, assessing climate risks and vulnerabilities, assessing mitigation potential and collecting sectoral scientific data.

4.3 TECHNOLOGY TRANSFER

Madagascar carried out its technological needs assessment in 2018. Technologies to combat climate change are available in Madagascar and some are developed at the national level (MIRR, SRI, SRA, agroforestry techniques, improved cookstoves, adapted crop varieties) through bias national research centers and universities. The development of endogenous technologies that adapt to the national context and are easy to disseminate will be more consistent with the recommendations of the PNCCR. In addition, the introduction of new innovative technologies developed outside the country will be leveraged through the development of win-win North-South, South-South, or tripartite (North-South-national) partnerships. The country's immediate needs in terms of technology transfer are:

  • Identification and capitalization of techniques, technologies and best practices in the development of mitigation technologies resulting from national research and development;

  • In-depth assessment of traditional and local knowledge that can contribute to reducing climate risks, reducing greenhouse gases and strengthening carbon sinks;

  • Strengthening the establishment of the national technical and operational knowledge sharing system, contributing to the development of collaboration and capacity building facilitating the adoption and promotion of innovative and transformative mitigation technologies with tangible impacts ;

  • The establishment of a monitoring-evaluation system to learn more about costs, results, and the process of planning and managing resources allocated to climate technologies.

4.4 GENDER CONSIDERATION, CLIMATE EMPOWERMENT AND PARTICIPATION AT ALL LEVELS

Madagascar aims to strengthen national capacities and mobilize more resources to consider the respect, promotion and consideration of its obligations concerning human rights, the right to health, the rights of indigenous peoples , local communities, migrants, children, people with disabilities and people in vulnerable situations, the right to development, as well as gender equality and equity between generations.

The roles of women and the most deprived, as agents of change within households, must be strengthened in the choice and promotion of adaptation and mitigation measures and technologies (by promoting energy less emitting cooking and lighting, or in the adoption of short cycle varieties, etc.). Madagascar will be actively involved in international approaches to gender integration, including the Action Plan for the Empowerment of Women and Gender Equality of the Paris Agreement.

Madagascar has developed structural efforts, and has proceeded with the development of strategic frameworks strengthening education, training and access to information. However, it is important to emphasize that the following elements are largely insufficient:

  • Development and implementation of public education and awareness programs;

  • Public access to information;

  • Public participation in the assessment of climate challenges and measures;

  • Training of scientific, technical and management personnel;

  • Cooperation and exchanges of experiences with a view to developing educational and awareness-raising materials.

    In its implementation, CDN2 also aims to develop an information network and strengthen the participation of all stakeholders: decision-makers, government institutions and agencies, cities, non-governmental organizations, civil society, the private sector, the media, research institutions and local communities.

4.5 DAMAGE LOSSES

4.5.1 IMPACT OF HAZARDS

4.5.1.1 CYCLONES AND FLOODS

  • Between 2007 and 2020:

    • 52 dead, 50 missing per year (58 deaths per year according to United Nations data published in 2020); 172,000 victims per hurricane season over the last 10 years;

    • 50,000 housing units destroyed per year;

    • 100,000 ha of agricultural plots destroyed per year;

  • Landslides and landslides, slowdown in road transport to the point of impassability on provincial and regional roads, slowdown in socio-economic exchanges, inflation, social conflicts;

  • Impossibility of operating airport infrastructure for up to several days and economic losses;

  • Impassibility of rail transport networks, social impacts caused by isolation and economic losses;

  • Index of loss of human life due to cyclones: 4.5 to 5 between 2007-2020 (objectives: 4 in 2020 according to CDN1);

4.5.1.2 DROUGHT

The food insecurity rate is 75.9% in Atsimo Andrefana, 63.4% in Androy and 53.4% in Anosy, the impact of drought is currently becoming more and more severe, around 1 .64 million people (37% of the population of the Great South) find themselves in high acute food insecurity, including more than 400,000 (9% of the population analyzed) in Phase 4 of the IPC (Emergency).

It is recommended to provide support focused on adapted cultivation techniques with agricultural inputs for the start of the major campaign. Assistance to strengthen livelihoods is recommended especially for households experiencing very severe insecurity.

4.5.2 VULNERABILITY

  • Limited capacity to anticipate and respond to climate disasters, despite the establishment of early warning systems (SAP): 27 observation stations (synoptic and hydrological) participating in climate and agrometeorological monitoring in 2017 ;

  • Policies not clearly defined, insufficient institutional capacity and lack of coordination: compartmentalization of interventions (separate institutions responsible for cyclone hazards, tsunamis, flooding of the Capital, and food insecurity);

  • Insufficient means for the operationalization of national standards developed to limit socio-economic losses;

  • Strategy to combat loss and damage (sustainable financing mechanism including an emergency fund and a compensation fund, emergency intervention mechanisms, capacity building related to crisis management, etc.) not developed ;

  • Only three operational centers for disaster risk management;

  • Early warning system covering only a few hazards (cyclones, floods, food insecurity in the South, flooding in the Capital). Other climatic hazards not covered: food and nutritional insecurity (national), episodes of drought, sanitary and phytosanitary surveillance;

  • Lack of means for the expansion and continuity of early warning system activities.

4.5.3 OBJECTIVES AND EDITION ACTIONS

  • Develop multi-risk early warning systems giving priority to cyclones, floods, drought, sanitary and phytosanitary surveillance;

  • Strengthen and update multi-risk early warning systems by integrating phytosanitary surveillance, agricultural warnings, drought alerts and food and nutritional surveillance;

  • Reduce the index of loss of life due to cyclones to 3 by 2030 through the effective applications of infrastructure standards and/or sectoral rules already established or initiated.

5. INSTITUTIONAL ARRANGEMENT FOR IMPLEMENTATION

5.1 HIGH-LEVEL STRATEGIC COORDINATION

The implementation of CDN2 will be carried out through a budgeted implementation plan. A simplified representation of the institutional arrangement is given in Figure 7, and the main institutions involved in the implementation of CDN2 are given in Table 2.

The Government, under the authority of the President of the Republic and the diligence of the Head of Government, constitutes the strategic coordination body. These institutions are responsible for developing the General Policy of the State and high-level arbitration. The Ministry responsible for Planning is responsible for coordinating the National Development Plan transcribing the implementation of the General Policy of the State into multi-annual programs of five years.

The implementation of these programs is included annually in the Finance Laws, the validation of which must go through the two parliamentary chambers, the National Assembly and the Senate. The operationalization of the CDN2 Implementation Plan will be done under the aegis of a Steering Committee, which will include government representations, representations of entities involved in the implementation of CDN2, as well as representatives of the National Committee on Climate Change (CNCC).

5.2 NATIONAL OPERATIONAL COORDINATION

The Ministry of Environment and Sustainable Development (MEDD), through the BNCCREDD+, is responsible for developing the budgeted and spatialized plan for the implementation of CDN2. The adoption of this plan will not exceed three months after the adoption of CDN2.

The MEDD was mandated by the Government of Madagascar to coordinate the operationalization of climate actions. The MEDD is also responsible for consolidating national inventories of GHGs and the impacts of climate hazards, as well as that of the achievements of measures carried out by the Government and other institutions involved in the implementation of climate actions. The MEDD is responsible for reporting (of these GHG emissions situations, climate risk and vulnerability situations, and corresponding achievements) to the UNFCCC Secretariat. The realization of these different attributions of the MEDD is carried out through the operationalization of the national and sectoral MRV systems which have recently been developed. This MRV system was set up in partnership with Conservation International, and in collaboration with the various ministerial institutions involved in climate actions. It was funded by the Capacity Building Initiative for the Transparency of the Paris Agreement (CBIT). Note, however, that the full operationalization of national and sectoral MRV systems still requires regulatory frameworks which should guarantee its continuous supply of information.

Periodic meetings of CNCC members will be held to monitor the achievements of climate actions within each department concerned with adaptation and mitigation. The CNCC was established in 2014 by decree no. 2014- 1588. It is a structure for sharing information and experiences, and also for dialogue and consultation, chaired by the Secretary General of the Ministry responsible for the Environment. It is made up of members appointed on the proposal of ministers of sectoral departments and representatives of other stakeholders in environmental protection and the fight against climate change. Among its responsibilities are, among other things, the proposal of measures or guidelines likely to strengthen the fight against climate change. As part of CDN2, the CNCC was consulted during its development. She will also be part of the steering committee for its implementation. The BNCC serves as secretary of the CNCC.

The minutes of the periodic CNCC meetings, consolidated by the MEDD, will be sent to the member of the Steering Committee for the implementation of CDN2, for their validation. The minutes thus validated will feed the information that goes up to the highest level of Government.

Mitigation co-benefits and description of measures and initiatives

UTCAF

The new Forest Policy developed in 2017 and the National Strategy for the Restoration of Forest Landscapes and green infrastructure in Madagascar plans to massively restore mangroves . A national mangrove restoration program will be developed, with a total target of 170,000 ha until 2030.

Improving the conservation of natural forests and the management of protected areas integrating climate refuge zones, the restoration and protection of riparian forest corridors, as well as the creation of wood supply zones, constitute measures recommended by the National Adaptation Plan.

The Malagasy State is also deploying efforts to restore forests to< meet the population’s wood needs. Madagascar is also committed to restoring, in 2020, 2.5 million ha of forest landscapes as part of the implementation of the “African Forest Landscape Restoration Initiative (AFR100)” and 4 million hectares by 2030 This commitment will serve as a target for accounting for other efforts that follow.

Since January 2019, forestry exploitation has been subject to a note suspending forestry exploitation and exports aimed at cleaning up the exploitation of forest products. This measure contributes to reducing emissions due to forest degradation and deforestation.

Madagascar is implementing a pilot program to ban the use of mercury in mining. This program will be implemented from 2023 and provides for the formalization of small farmers who will be made responsible for limiting the impacts of their activities on deforestation and forest and land degradation. These actions will strengthen ecosystem services and at the same time strengthen carbon sinks.

Various territorial planning and development programs are emerging, among others: restoration of watersheds upstream of agricultural areas, sustainable agriculture using the landscape approach, ecological villages, fight against endangered species and habitat conservation, conservation program for key species with economic values.

Agriculture

Madagascar joins the African initiative aimed at the adoption of climate-smart agricultural techniques or CSA by 2025. The national objective is to strengthen the resilience of production systems and promote sustainable agricultural practices allowing mitigate greenhouse gas emissions . 50% of producers will have access to agricultural services, i.e. 1.2 million family farm units.

Madagascar's PNA strengthens the development of Integrated Models of Resilient Agriculture that emits less methane and continues to promote the Intensive Rice Farming System and the Improved Rice Farming System. This initiative was developed as part of the implementation of the NAPA and was adopted by the Africa Rice initiative. The PNA also plans to valorize by-products and develop value chains to improve income to limit the expansion of the conversion of forests and wetlands. The PNA also recommends mixed production systems combining crops and livestock, the implementation of a pastoral water program, the diversification of fodder plants and a gradual evolution towards extensive livestock models.

Various current or contemporary initiatives with the implementation of CDN2 will certainly have impacts in terms of reducing GHG emissions: Improvement of rice productivity (mitigation of GHG emissions and reduction in the conversion of forest land, meadows and wetlands in rice plots); improved variety (resistant to hazards and short cycle); anti-erosion programs; land use planning using a landscape approach (reduction of deforestation and forest degradation, sustainable agriculture); development of sustainable agricultural value chains and sectors; protection and rehabilitation of soils and improvement of food security; improving adaptation capacities and resilience to climate change; agricultural growth and land security. The estimation of emissions reduction projections resulting from these initiatives contains many uncertainties; but they will be estimated in the CDN2 Agriculture sector mitigation scenario.

Water resources

The National Strategy for Integrated Water Resources Management, which should be broken down into development plans and integrated management of water resources (SDAGIRE), sets, as an objective in 2030, the reduction of evapotranspiration and runoff (reforestation and protection of watersheds), the fight against drought through the protection and restoration of water-related ecosystems, and the increase in storage through, among other things, infiltration basins on slopes watersheds.

For the PNA, the Great South is the priority region for the development and implementation of SDAGIRE. The PNA also recommends, as an adaptation measure for this sector, the restoration of mangroves and the preservation of wetlands.

Energy

In 2021, to replace dependence on charcoal and firewood which constitute the cooking energy of 97% of households in 2020, the Government of Madagascar reduced value added taxes on butane gas. The same year, a public-private partnership was concluded which aims, among other things, a 9% reduction in the price of butane gas and measures to facilitate access to this cleaner energy.

Waste

Madagascar continues its slow progress towards reducing emissions due to industrial emissions. An initiative aimed at circularity in the textile and clothing industry is emerging, targeting three pilot factories.

Madagascar is losing a lot of water resources due to poor sanitation. The Government plans to establish water, sanitation and hygiene and waste management infrastructure.

In the Water Resources sector, the PNA prescribes the renewal of the pipeline systems of all major cities.

Others

Madagascar is in the process of tripling the surface area of its marine protected areas. This program will strengthen ecosystem services and guarantee the connectivity of marine habitats and the exchange of gene flows which will promote the adaptation of ecosystems and its components. Although emissions linked to the destruction of marine habitats were not counted during greenhouse gas inventories, these measures contribute to the maintenance and strengthening of carbon stocks. This program is consistent with the objective of the PNA concerning Coastal Zones, and with its objective of operationalizing a sustainable fishing program.

6.2.8 HOW THE PREPARATION OF THE NATIONALLY DETERMINED CONTRIBUTION WAS INFORMED BY THE RESULTS OF THE GLOBAL REVIEW, IN ACCORDANCE WITH ARTICLE 4, PARAGRAPH 9 OF THE PARIS AGREEMENT

In 2020, Madagascar remains a carbon sink, sequestering 25,527 Gg eq. CO2. This sequestration capacity is gradually lost. Just after the year 2025, two years after the end of the first Global Assessment in 2023,

Madagascar will become a net source of greenhouse gases. Apart from the loss of forest covers which are the main sources of the LULUCF sector, the most significant increases in emissions relate to the Waste sectors, the Energy sector, then Industrial Processes and Product Use and Agriculture. The source categories at the level of these sectors will form the targets of the national emissions reduction objectives in NDC2.

As the Global Assessment also considers adaptation, means of implementation and support, as well as efforts to learn about, minimize and take into account loss and damage, CDN2 will also include components addressing these aspects.

6.2.9 METHODOLOGICAL AND METRIC APPROACHES USED FOR ESTIMATION AND ACCOUNTING OF EMISSIONS AND ABSORPTION OF GREENHOUSE GAS

The national greenhouse gas inventories carried out in Madagascar (CNI, DCN, TCN and CDN1) serve as the basis for the development of the CDN2. These inventories use the Revised 1996 IPCC Guidelines, supported by the IPCC Recommendations on Good Practice and Uncertainty Management for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories (GPG 2000) and the Good Practice Recommendations for the LULUCF sector (GPG 2003). All greenhouse gas inventories at sector level are Tier 1.

Madagascar is currently developing its first Biennial Updated Report and its Fourth National Communication. The results of the inventories corresponding to these national reports will make it possible to update greenhouse gas estimates and emissions. They will feed into the update of the CDN from the year 2025.

6.2.11 USE OF VOLUNTARY COOPERATION UNDER ARTICLE 6 OF THE PARIS AGREEMENT

Madagascar plans to develop national frameworks to benefit from the voluntary cooperation mechanisms of Article 6 of the Paris Agreement. National capacities will be strengthened to facilitate access to climate-smart technologies, for equitable sharing of benefits, for mitigation activities promoting access to sustainable development, and for access to information. National capacities (scientific and technical, institutional) for accounting for emissions and emission reductions, the system for accounting for emissions and emission reductions are to be developed and progressively improved from this CDN2.

6.2.12 FAIR AND AMBITIOUS

Madagascar is a less developed country that has not historically contributed to global warming. The country remains a greenhouse gas sink in 2020. A priori, and according to the Lima Call for Action, it seems unfair to ask a country like Madagascar to contribute to global efforts and set objectives quantified or quantifiable emissions reductions.

Madagascar is an island nation with maritime territories endowed with archipelagos submersible by the increase in sea level. The Government of Madagascar is committed to the fight against the harmful effects of global warming, as a very vulnerable and very exposed to extreme climatic events and slow-onset events. The measures to combat climate change proposed in CDN2 make it possible to strengthen the environmental integrity of the nation and secure the ecosystem services essential to its sustainable and equitable development. Furthermore, the Government of Madagascar is aware that current global efforts to reduce greenhouse gases are largely insufficient and cause losses and damage, particularly for the most vulnerable countries such as Africa and island nations. The Republic of Madagascar is thus making its contributions to limit the global increase in temperature to 1.5°C by 2100.

In the context of the global pandemic due to COVID-19, Madagascar continues to demonstrate its desire for solidarity and its good faith in all aspects of sustainability and equity. Madagascar would like to strengthen the awakening of collective consciences for the respect of commitments, and appeals to those responsible for global changes to lead the way and undertake actions corresponding to their responsibilities and capacities.

6.2.13 PROGRESS IN RELATION TO CONTRIBUTION

DETERMINED AT THE PREVIOUS NATIONAL LEVEL (ARTICLE 4, PARAGRAPH 3 OF THE PARIS AGREEMENT), PASSAGE

PROGRESSIVE TOWARDS ECONOMY-WIDE EMISSION REDUCTION TARGETS, WITH REGARD TO DIFFERENT NATIONAL SITUATIONS

Sources and greenhouse gases and their impacts

The CDN1 only considered the following sectors: Agriculture, Energy LULUCF and Waste. CDN2 included the Industrial Processes and Product Use sector. Which raised the ambitions compared to the sources.

Ambitions to reduce emissions and strengthen absorption capacities

In 2030, the CDN2 targets a 28% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions, or 32,642 Gg eq. CO2.

In addition to this reduction in emissions, CDN2 aims to strengthen its greenhouse gas absorption capacity by around 20%, or -37,809 Gg eq. CO2 from additional sequestration.

In 2030, the CDN1 targeted a 14% reduction in direct greenhouse gas emissions, equivalent to 29,990 Gg eq. CO2 and additional sequestration of -61,000 Gg eq. CO2, i.e. 32% reinforcement of carbon sinks. The mitigation ambitions presented in CDN2 appear to have regressed compared to those of CDN1; but they are realistic and achievable, and reflect the realities of current and projected sectoral policies and socio-economic contexts. Concerning the LULUCF sector, the mitigation potentials of the mitigation actions were not fully included in the CDN2 since these potentials will not be fully expressed by 2030. Only 25-30% of the mitigation results of the LULUCF sector are expressed here.

Other progressions compared to CDN1

The CDN2 includes sectoral and regulatory policy development activities which should enable the country to gradually expand its climate actions, in parallel with its sustainable development objectives. Furthermore, the implementation of many climate actions are cross-sectoral and are actions to scale up initiatives already started before 2020.

The operationalization of national and sectoral MRV systems will make it possible to collect data which will allow the country to gradually move towards a political reflex to reduce emissions on an economy-wide scale. To this end, as stipulated by Article 4, paragraph 5, of the Paris Agreement, as a developing country, the Government of Madagascar intends to benefit from the support of the other Parties to the Paris Agreement for the achievement objectives contained in its CDN2.
