1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Mombyky | Executive Summary

The Republic of Paraguay has submitted to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), its Nationally Determined Contribution Intent (INDC) in 2015, which became the Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC for its acronym in English) in 2016, from the entry into force of the Paris Agreement, ratified by the country, through Law No. 5681/16.

This exercise to Update the NDC and the instruments generated within the framework of the same to 2021, were established based on the guidelines of the National Development Plan Paraguay 2030 (PND Paraguay 2030), the National Policy on Climate Change and other sectoral, national and international policies, considered as pillars of the commitments assumed by the country.

In addition, the update includes the identification of transversal axes for the generation of the instruments, covering the management of climate risks, regulatory aspects, the gender perspective, attention to indigenous peoples, climate empowerment and the promotion of the Goals. Sustainable Development (SDG) by 2030.

On the other hand, considering the national circumstances, and the concepts of equity and climate justice, as well as the sovereign right of sustainable development of the countries, this NDC reaffirms that:

  • Given the negligible historical contribution of the Republic of Paraguay to global GHG emissions (≤0.01%) (Ritchie and Roser, 2020), despite its high vulnerability to climate change, exacerbated by its status as a landlocked country (SEAM / UNDP / GEF, 2017), adaptation is seen as a priority national approach. Thus, according to findings reported in the scientific study "State of the Climate in Paraguay" (Grassi, 2020), the year 2019 was the hottest in national history, with +1.5 and +1.7 ° C with respect to temperature 1961-1990 and pre-industrial era mean, respectively. For its part, the average annual precipitation has increased by 200 mm during the last 70 years.

  • Given the country's contribution to the achievement of global objectives such as food security, in accordance with the vision of the PND Paraguay 2030, it is envisioned as a global platform for food production in the context of its sustainable development.

Flaunting its mitigation efforts through good practices and plausible technological innovations, the country assumed since 2015 and reaffirms in this update, the commitment to reduce projected emissions by 20% in the BAU (Business as usual) scenario by 2030 , 10% corresponding to a fraction conditioned to the international provision of the means of implementation and the other 10% to an unconditional fraction, based on the use of its own resources.

In compliance with the five-year update, called for by Art. 4.9 of the Paris Agreement, this exercise to update the NDC to 2021 is based on multisectoral participatory processes, for the construction of the 2 Components: Adaptation and Mitigation to Change Climate.

It should be noted that according to Art. 5 of the Paris Agreement, in this update of the NDC, through the so-called “Nature-based Solutions” (NBS), the preponderant role of ecosystems and their environmental services is recognized , mainly that of forests, grasslands, wetlands and aquifers, for adaptation and mitigation to climate change, by contributing to food security, disaster risk reduction, carbon sequestration and the resilience of countries with abundant natural capital, such as the Republic of Paraguay.

FIRST COMMUNICATION OF ADAPTATION

By virtue of Art. 7 of the Paris Agreement, the First Adaptation Communication is reported, at the time of establishing a total of 25 objectives, with their lines of action, gaps and needs by 2030, in 7 prioritized sectors.

  1. Resilient Communities and Cities, with 5 objectives aimed at citizen participation and municipal management, resilient infrastructures and green areas in cities, as well as promoting sustainable tourism and the natural and cultural heritage of Protected Wild Areas (ASP).

  2. Health and Epidemiology, with 4 objectives aimed at strengthening health systems in the face of health emergencies, derived from meteorological phenomena, extreme weather events and emerging diseases.

  3. Ecosystems and Biodiversity, with 2 objectives aimed at the sustainable management of ecosystems and Protected Wild Areas (ASP), for the provision of ecosystem services and the protection of biodiversity.

  4. Energy, with 3 objectives aimed at improving the distribution capacity of electricity services in vulnerable communities, as well as promoting actions for the conservation and restoration of water basins in recharge areas for the generation of hydroelectric energy.

  5. Agricultural, Forestry and Food Security Production, with 5 objectives aimed at promoting adaptive capacity through good agroforestry practices, technified production, improved crops, to promote global food security, access to markets by farmers. family farmers and indigenous peoples, as well as access to agro-meteorological information for timely decision-making.

  6. Water Resources, with 4 objectives aimed at the management and access to safe water in the face of the threat of scarcity, as well as the protection and restoration of wetlands and springs.

  7. Transport, with 2 objectives aimed at the sustainable management of cross-border rivers and the development of alternatives for the transport of passengers and goods at a national and international level.

2. INTRODUCTION

As of the ratification of the Paris Agreement, by the Republic of Paraguay in 2016, the generation of various public policy instruments began, including Strategies and Plans for Adaptation and Mitigation to Climate Change. Also, since then, actions have been taken to identify potential sources or entities that provide the means of implementation for the NDC.

Consecutively, between 2020 and 2021, in the health and socio-economic context generated by the SARS-COVID19 pandemic, the process of updating the NDC of the Republic of Paraguay to 2030 began, mainly covering the following sections:

  • Cross-cutting axes and actions, which include approaches and relevant social segments, in the process of participatory construction of climate commitments.

  • The First Adaptation Communication, which represents an important advance in the identification of ambitious objectives for the adaptation of 7 sectors considered a priority at the national level, due to their impact on the socio-cultural, economic and environmental development of the country. On the other hand, for these priority sectors, 25 objectives are established by 2030, with their respective lines of action, gaps and needs, which aim to reduce vulnerability and increase resilience to the negative impacts of climate change.

  • Information for Transparency, Update of the BAU (Business As Usual) Scenario, Sector Plans and Climate Change Mitigation Scenarios to 2030.

  • Means of implementation, which include: the need to build and strengthen capacities, climate finance, development and transfer of technologies, as resources or conditioning factors for the achievement of the objectives and proposed goals.

  • Aspects for continuous improvement, with respect to the national commitments outlined in 2015 and those to be reported next. The context in which this update is developed conditions some substantial differences in relation to the INDC of 2015, such as:

    • Review of the priority sectors and objectives of the Adaptation Component, which is reaffirmed as the national priority approach, given the country's high vulnerability to climate change as established in the National Plan for Adaptation to Climate Change (SEAM / UNDP / GEF, 2017), despite the fact that national contributions to global emissions of Greenhouse Gases (GHG) are negligible.

    • Relevance to elucidate that although in the First NDC, adaptation has been affirmed as the priority national approach, in this update plans with specific sectoral mitigation measures are proposed, since all the countries ratifying the Paris Agreement, taking into account Considering their national circumstances, they should consider adaptation and mitigation of climate change, as complementary but not subordinate components. Therefore, the Sectoral Plans for Climate Change Mitigation (PMCC) constitute “tangible” proposals within the scope of mitigation expressed in 2015, which extends to the sectors mentioned in the methodological guides of the Intergovernmental Panel of Experts on Climate Change ( IPCC) to carry out the National Inventories of Greenhouse Gases (INGEI).

      In addition, it should be considered that the methodological approach carried out obeys the different required monitoring processes: Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) for Adaptation and Monitoring, Reporting and Verification (MRV) for Mitigation. However, this should not be interpreted as a contradiction to Art. 7 of the Paris Agreement, since unfailingly many of the measures identified present co-benefits for both approaches, in the context of the country's sustainable development.

    • On the part of the Mitigation Component, although an increase in the goal is not explicitly proposed in relation to the 20% committed in 2015, the adjustment of the baseline implies a lower projection of GHG emissions by 2030, taking into account the lower projected economic expansion (expected growth, according to the historical average GDP growth ≤3.17% during the last decade, instead of the 6.60% considered in 2015 according to the Paraguay 2030 PND). This implies that, although a more ambitious mitigation goal is not proposed per se than that of 2015, the unconditional proportion of 10% contemplates a greater conjunctural challenge for the country in terms of:

    • Use of the public budget and management of international resources for climate action.

    • Need for economic reactivation and promotion of green recovery options in the face of the impacts of the SARS-COVID19 pandemic, in the context of sustainable development.

    Finally, it should be noted that the advances envisioned by the Republic of Paraguay, such as the updating of the base data and the estimation of the potential impact of good practices and technological innovations by 2030, through the consolidation of the institutional work team, technical bases, subsequently requiring strategic alliances and joint efforts by all national sectors, in order to implement the NDC and fulfill the objectives of the Paris Agreement:

    • Limit to the year 2100, the increase in the global average temperature below 2 ° C, and if possible 1.5 ° C, with respect to pre-industrial levels, recognizing that this would considerably reduce the adverse effects of the climate change;

    • Increase the capacity to adapt to the adverse effects of climate change, promoting climate resilience and development with low GHG emissions, in a way that does not compromise food production and,

    • Bring financial flows to a level compatible with the climate-resilient and low-GHG development trajectory.

4.1 COUNTRY CONTEXT

In the First NDC, in force since 2016, adaptation to climate change is defined as a national priority, and 9 priority sectors are named, selected in accordance with the definitions of the National Development Plan of Paraguay 2030, due to their importance for socio-economic, environmental and cultural development. These sectors have served as the basis for the Strategy (2015) and the National Plan for Adaptation to Climate Change (2017), developed in subsequent years.

For the present update of the NDC, the priority sectors are regrouped into 7, for which the aim is to increase the capacity to adapt to the present and future impacts of climate change, based on the definition of 25 objectives by 2030, with their respective lines of action, needs and gaps. This new scope in the definition of objectives for priority adaptation sectors is developed in accordance with Article 4, paragraph 3, of the Paris Agreement, which “urges countries to increase their ambition progressively in each presentation of the NDC , taking into account national circumstances ”.

Additionally, in line with Article 7, paragraph 10, of the Paris Agreement, which “invites the parties to present and periodically update the adaptation communication, with information on priorities, support needs and actions implemented at the national level”, Paraguay presents as part of this NDC, its First Adaptation Communication. It was structured based on the content established for said document, in Decision 9 / CMA.1, developed in 2018 for the Conference of the Parties (COP).

In turn, the objectives, lines of action, needs and gaps contribute to other international normative frameworks, such as the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, the Convention for Biological Diversity, the Convention to Combat Desertification, the Sendai Framework. , Warsaw Mechanism for Loss and Damage, and the Ramsar Convention on Wetlands of International Importance. It should be noted that several of the objectives were considered taking into account a gender and indigenous peoples perspective, as well as possible co-benefits for the mitigation of climate change.

This document is the result of the technical review of available official information, consultation work involving governing institutions of the different public sectors, and bilateral meetings with experts, members of the CNCC and members of the "ad hoc Table". "from NDC. On the other hand, it also includes consultations with representatives of municipalities and communities established in different ecoregions of the country.

As of the presentation of this NDC to the UNFCCC, Paraguay will initiate a broad dialogue, from the national level to the sub-national governments, in order to consolidate the institutional arrangements necessary for the definition of a Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) system. ) focused on measuring the objectives of adaptation to climate change, and establishing a roadmap for their implementation.

4.2 NATIONAL CIRCUMSTANCES AND INSTITUTIONAL ARRANGEMENTS

4.2.1 International Frameworks and Conventions

Paraguay, as a member country of the United Nations, in 2015 approved the 2030 agenda for the member states of the United Nations (UN) for Sustainable Development. Within the framework of this agenda, 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) were adopted, which guide public development policies and serve as a guide for planning and implementing actions against climate change.

In 1995, the Ramsar Convention on the Protection of Wetlands of International Importance entered into force in Paraguay, signed in 1971.

On the other hand, incorporating into the commitments as a Party to the UNFCCC, Paraguay by Law No. 253/93 “Approving the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD), adopted during the United Nations Conference on Environment and Development - the Earth Summit -, held in the city of Rio de Janeiro, Brazil and by Law No. 970/96 “Approving the United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification (UNCCD), in countries affected by drought severe or desertification, particularly in Africa ”, these commitments are transversal and complement the management of climate change in areas that are directly affected by its impacts.

Through Decree No. 5965/16 Paraguay, the objective, priorities and global goals of the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction 2015-2030 are adopted ”. The National Plan for the implementation of the Sendai Framework (2018-2022), constitutes the multisectoral planning tool in the short, medium and long term to achieve the goal and the seven objectives of the Sendai Framework, which allows progress towards the strengthening of resilience in Paraguay.

4.2.2. The Warsaw International Mechanism for Loss and Damage

This mechanism is established within the UNFCCC to strengthen the capacities of the Parties to face the losses and damages associated with the impacts of climate change in an integrated and coherent way, through the understanding of risks, increased dialogue and the promotion of mechanisms to facilitate technical and financial support.

Along the same lines, Paraguay recognizes the need to promote its participation in the discussions and negotiations of said mechanism, in order to carry out actions that address and minimize losses and damages related to the adverse effects of climate change, including to extreme and slow-onset weather events, causing forced displacement and migration.

NATIONAL POLICY INSTRUMENTS WITH A FOCUS ON ADAPTATION TO CLIMATE CHANGE

The Paraguayan Government has been working on instruments and tools such as policies, national plans of action and projects, to promote the adoption of a vision of long-term sustainable development.

In September 2016, the National Congress of the Republic of Paraguay sanctioned the ratification of the Paris Agreement, through Law No. 5681/16. The following year, the National Climate Change Law was enacted, promulgated by the Executive Power, through Law No. 5875/17, which aims to establish the general regulatory framework that allows planning and responding, urgently, adequately , coordinated and sustained, in the face of the impacts of climate change, in coherence with the Paris Agreement and in line with the SDGs.

In 2014, the Paraguay 2030 National Development Plan was developed, which is the document that defines the strategic axes and objectives, the policy priorities and the lines of action for the inclusive and sustained development of Paraguay, including among its axes poverty reduction, social development and inclusive economic growth; on the other hand, as transversal lines, territorial ordering and development, and environmental sustainability are defined. Framed by these guidelines, MADES is responsible for providing support tools to promote adaptation mechanisms, as well as mitigation, providing planning and management instruments to governments at the national and subnational level, in order to reduce the impacts. of climate change.

In 2015, the National Strategy for Adaptation to Climate Change (ENACC) was developed, which defined principles and action frameworks for increasing resilience. They were used in 2016, as the basis for the development of the National Plan for Adaptation to Climate Change (PNACC), which identified priority sectors and established an operational structure in order to become an articulating instrument of Paraguayan public policy. in the field of adaptation.

4.3 VULNERABILITIES TO CLIMATE VARIABILITY AND CLIMATE CHANGE

The study of the "State of the Climate in Paraguay 2019" (Grassi, 2020), provides evidence that climate change is accelerating in the country, and this fact, beyond representing only an environmental problem, constitutes a threat to development sustainable country.

The increase in temperature in Paraguay can be observed in different time scales, not only in the values of the annual average temperatures, as is the case of the last five-year period 2015-2019; the last decade 2010-2019 and the last 30 years from 1990-2019, which were the hottest compared to their predecessors since 1960.

Average annual rainfall increased 200 mm in the last 70 years, this represents a trend of approximately 3 mm / year. The floods of the Paraguay River in Asunción were more frequent in the last 40 years (1980-2019) since 1904, in which there are records. Some cities have been experiencing floods in periods of 10 or 12 years, however, in recent years they occur more frequently. The rains and consequent floods especially affect the Eastern Region and the Chaco, and occur between the months of October to May. According to the National Plan for Adaptation to Climate Change (2017), floods affect riverside cities such as Concepción, Asunción, Alberdi, Pilar and Encarnación. However, in recent years floods have also been observed in different areas of the Central Chaco.

The number of heat waves tripled in the country in the last 40 years (1980-2019), severe droughts were more frequent in this century than in the past. The year 2019 was the hottest in the history of the country, with an average annual temperature of 24.3 ° C and +1.5 ° C above the average of the standard climatological normal temperature of 1961-1990, as well as spring this year was the hottest (26 ° C) in fifty years. Additionally, in the same year, extreme events such as agricultural droughts, floods, extreme heat waves, hydrological droughts, forest fires and severe storms have occurred, events that have had a strong impact on the economy (Grassi, 2020).

The information collected from local actors was obtained from semi-structured interviews where their perception of the impact of climate change at the local level was investigated, taking as axis of analysis the priority sectors established in the process of updating the NDC. This process was carried out through interviews with 34 key local actors in the 27 selected municipalities (See Annex 1.2).

Of those interviewed, practically all of them identified the increase in temperature as a slow-onset phenomenon linked to climate change. Regarding the type of extreme events, the majority referred to intense and concentrated rains as relatively recent events associated with climate change. In addition, it is mentioned that the floods, which were more gradual before, are now more abrupt and require quick reactions. On the other hand, it is perceived that periods of drought are now more frequent and intense, and facilitate the spread of fires, which are equally intense and more frequent.

On the other hand, a correspondence is observed between the threats, vulnerabilities and climatic risks detected in the Disaster Risk Atlas of the Republic of Paraguay (SEN, 2018) and the events perceived by local actors. It is worth mentioning the identification of intense and concentrated rains, since in many of these municipalities agricultural activities are carried out, and this type of event not only generates floods, but impacts on the soil and crops.

After the interviews carried out, the need to promote discussions that allow the identification of the effects that climate change can have on the different groups that are part of society is concluded, which, together with the information generated by the institutions and academia, allow to guide the local planning process and decision-making.

4.4 OBJECTIVES, LINES OF ACTION, GAPS AND NEEDS

4.4.1 Methodology used to establish objectives

The definition of the objectives and lines of action was based on the review and update of the existing official information, which was complemented by participatory processes with reference institutions and sector experts. Based on this, an analysis and crossover of the sectorial information and other cross-cutting issues (donor, geographic scope, start and end date, executing institution, benefiting sectors, etc.) was carried out, and a database of data with the initiatives consulted, which are currently being carried out in the country.

From the aforementioned process, a total of 25 objectives are obtained that address the different territorial, socioeconomic and environmental vulnerabilities and opportunities in the face of climate change for the 7 priority sectors, distributed in Resilient Communities and Cities (5), Health and epidemiology (4) , Ecosystems and Biodiversity (2), Energy (3), Agricultural Production, Forestry and Food Security (5), Water Resources (4) and Transportation (2).

In turn, these sectoral objectives integrate in a transversal way the gender perspective, indigenous peoples, risk management, action for climate empowerment, regulatory aspects, research and development, as well as their alignment with compliance with frameworks. national and international regulations (PND 2030, ODS, Sendai Framework).

OBJECTIVE 4. Strengthen the capacity to adapt to climate change in articulated groups of citizens.

OBJECTIVE 5. Strengthen the resilience of the tourism sector to the negative impacts of climate change.

OBJECTIVE 6. Strengthen the health response capacity to face emergency situations related to extreme meteorological and climatic phenomena.

OBJECTIVE 10. Increase the climate resilience of those ecosystems in which socio-economic and cultural practices are carried out, based on the use of Nature-based Solutions (NBS).

ENERGY SECTOR

TRANSPORTATION SECTOR

OBJECTIVE 24. Properly plan and manage the navigability of transboundary rivers in times of low water and drought.

OBJECTIVE 25. Direct the development of the different transport infrastructures, to increase the resilience of the sector to the adverse effects of climate change, facilitating the mobilization of people and national and international trade.

5. MITIGATION IN THE FACE OF CLIMATE CHANGE

5.1 COUNTRY CONTEXT

The Republic of Paraguay, by the current National Constitution, is a Social State of Law, unitary and decentralized with a democratic, representative, pluralistic and participatory system, with distinguished natural capital and an economic system of free trade, which favors free movement of financial resources and the movement of capital, as an open market economy for both import and export. Primary production (agricultural and livestock) represents the base of socio-economic activity, which is boosted by agro-industrialization and export of commodities, contributing annually up to 50% to the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) (Investor, 2015) . In addition to this, there are other national circumstances, such as a highly renewable energy matrix,

These national circumstances, typical of the development of countries with abundant natural capital, are reflected in the historical net emissions profile. Thus, for example, based on the latest National Inventory of Greenhouse Gases (INGEI), officially reported in the Second Biennial Update Report (IBA2), for the year 2015 (last year of the time series addressed), total national emissions amounted to 75,099.9 Gg CO2 eq, of which ≈32% corresponded to absorptions, resulting in a net annual emission of ≈68%.

It should be noted that in the time series (1990-2015) considered until 2013, the Sector of Land Use, Changes in Land Use and Forestry (LULUCF) represented the highest percentage of contribution to the country's net emissions (See graph 4) However, this sector experienced a significant reduction in its emissions in 2015, after which it was possible to obtain a historical recognition of payment for results for the reduction of emissions from deforestation and forest degradation (REDD + ), through the Green Climate Fund (GCF).

Graph 3 Profile of historical net emissions (Gg CO2 eq), reported by Paraguay for the time series 1990-2015.

However, considering the concepts of equity, climate justice and the sovereign right to sustainable development of the countries, the following should be highlighted:

  • Very small historical contribution of the Republic of Paraguay to global GHG emissions (≤0.01%) (Ritchie and Roser, 2020) despite its high vulnerability to climate change.

  • Contribution of the country to the achievement of global objectives such as food security, in accordance with the vision of the National Development Plan Paraguay 2030, which envisions it as a global food platform, in the context of its sustainable development.

Thus, aiming at sustainable development, under the Paris Agreement, Paraguay has committed since its first NDC in 2015, a goal of reducing 20% of projected emissions by 2030, being 10% uncon / conditioned and 10% conditional, to the international provision of the means of implementation.

To achieve the goal, the country has built public policy instruments, based on the Paraguay 2030 PND, the National Climate Change Policy (PNCC) and the National Environmental Policy (PAN, in the process of updating), among which are find the Mitigation Strategy (2014), the National Climate Change Mitigation Plan and its 7 Action Programs (2017) and the current Sector Plans, later referred to as Climate Change Mitigation Plans (PMCC), which present 45 measures tangible and referential measures for national climate action on mitigation, for the next few years.

"Deviation of the level of emissions from the national BAU (Business as Usual) scenario"

b) Mitigation goal

20% reduction in BAU emissions projected to 2030, being the:

- Target Conditional on external financing: 10% of the reduction.

- Unconditioned Goal: 10% of the reduction.

c) Starting year and implementation period of the NDC

2014 (start year) -2030 (end year). In 2014, it was selected as the beginning of the implementation of the NDC, aligned with the implementation period of the National Development Plan of Paraguay to 2030.

Emission value (national total) of GHGs projected in the BAU Scenario by 2030: 102,542 Gg CO2 eq

Emission levels to be achieved, according to the mitigation goal:

-Unconditioned (-10% reduction): reduce the emission of 10,254 Gg CO2 eq, not exceeding 92,287 Gg CO2 eq by 2030.

-Conditional (-10% reduction): additionally reduce the emission of 10,254 Gg CO2 eq, not exceeding the emission of 82,033.6 Gg CO2 eq by 2030, together with the unconditional goal.

However, the Republic of Paraguay clarifies that the projected value of GHG emissions in the BAU scenario to 2030 is indicative , still reserving the right to review / update it in its First Biennial Transparency Report (IBT). It is also pointed out that the progress indicator, for monitoring the achievement of the mitigation goal of the NDC, will be the level of annual net emissions, reported in the next INGEI.

In line with the National Climate Change Mitigation Plan and its Action Programs (SEAM / UNDP, 2017) approved by SEAM Resolution No. 275/2018, during the current exercise of updating its NDC, the Republic of Paraguay has undertaken the preparation of Climate Change Mitigation Plans (PMCC), in the 5 sectors of the INGEI. Therefore, the impacts of the PMCC will also be reviewed and / or updated in the First IBT of the country.

In line with the principle of continuous improvement, in accordance with the greater accuracy and precision of the estimates of net GHG emissions, to be subsequently reported in the INGEI of the Third Biennial Update Report (IBA3), of the Fourth National Communication (CN4) and of the First IBT, the reference values, expressed in this NDC, are expected to be updated.

Additionally, future methodological improvement may consider, among other aspects, the inclusion of GHG goal / s, categories and / or coverage not yet reported in the previous INGEI.

In addition, given the conditional portion (10%) of the mitigation goal, the Republic of Paraguay reserves the right to adjust its level of ambition according to the support received and its national circumstances, as well as the decisions / negotiations derived from the Paris Agreement (for example, in relation to the regulations on the International Carbon Market, referred to in Article 6).

16 years in total, counting from December 2014 to December 2030, in line with the provisions of the Paraguay 2030 National Development Plan.

The NDC of the Republic of Paraguay preliminarily commits a single annual mitigation goal to 2030, without including a mid-term reference indicator.

3. Scope and coverage:
to. General description of the mitigation goal, including geographic and reference framework.

The mitigation goal, consisting of reducing the projected GHG emissions by 20% in the BAU scenario by 2030 (being conditioned and not conditioned in equal parts), extends to the entire national territory and the sectors of quantification of the INGEI according to the methodological guides of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC).
b. IPCC gases, categories and sinks, addressed in the NDC

The GHG, whose emissions / removals are considered in the INGEI and this NDC are: CO2 (Carbon dioxide), CH4 (Methane), N2O (Nitrous oxide), HFC (Hydrofluorocarbons), PFC (Perfluorocarbons) and SF6 (Hexafluoride of sulfur).

For their part, the emission categories and sinks considered correspond mainly to those historically reported in the INGEI.

Regarding anthropogenic disturbances, the country excludes emissions from forest fires from its NDC, because it does not have the capacity to accurately estimate or mitigate them.
c) Secondary mitigation benefits resulting from the Party's adaptation measures and / or economic diversification plans

Although there may be adaptation sectors / measures, as well as economic diversification plans that derive in mitigation benefits to the country (eg With the Nature-based Solutions Approach-NBS, for the prioritized sector of Ecosystems and Biodiversity), the They are indicated in the specific chapters of this update of the NDC, without implying a commitment to jointly approach by virtue of the different monitoring processes required. 5.3 BAU SCENARIOS (“BUSINESS AS USUAL”) FROM PARAGUAY TO 2030

The “Business as Usual” (BAU) Scenarios correspond to the scenarios where all the policies and measures applied, approved and planned will be excluded from the year chosen as the starting point. Emissions / removals are projected based on macro-economic variables such as Gross Domestic Product (GDP), population growth and other variables that correlate with GHG emissions / removals, both sectoral (see section 5.3.1.) as nationals (see section 5.3.2.).

The influence of the driver variables on GHG emissions / removals were estimated based on the goodness of fit parameters, produced by the econometric models used:

  • Theoretical model type "Cobb Douglas" by the method of Feasible Generalized Least Squares (top - down approach), for all projections (national and sectoral, except those of the Energy sector and the Transport subsector), using the "R" software (R Foundation for Statistical Computing, 2021).

  • General Equilibrium Model, for more detailed projections of the Energy Sector (bottom up approach), through the use of LEAP software (Stockholm Environment Institute, 2021).

5.4.5 Priority measures for the Energy and Transport Sector Climate Change Mitigation Plan (PMCC-ENyTR)

Taking into account national circumstances, with an energy matrix highly dependent on renewable sources (mainly still on forest biomass and a world-leading “clean” hydroelectric production), in addition to the majority share of sectoral GHG emissions (> 80% , based on the transport category or subsector), the energy sector mitigation measures were specifically surveyed for both the energy sector (see section 5.4.5.1) and transport (see section 5.4.5.2.); The sectoral mitigation scenarios have been constructed to contrast them with the sectoral BAU scenario projected to 2030, using the LEAP tool.

It should be noted that the measures, assumptions and scenarios of the energy sector were proposed in close connection with the public policies “applied or approved” (For example, the National Development Plan Paraguay 2030, the National Energy Policy 2040, the exemption tax on the importation of electric vehicles through Law No. 5183/14 and the update of the Transportation Master Plan (MOPC, 2018) etc.).

5.4.5.2 Priority mitigation measures for the Transport Sector (TR).

For this sector, through the mechanism of surveys and workshops with sector experts, the following mitigation measures were prioritized by 2030 (4):

  • TR.1. The increasing substitution of fossil fuels for biofuels (depending on the type of engine, up to 7.5% addition to diesel and 27.5% addition to gasoline). This measure, as well as that of the use of better quality fossil fuels, implies the innovation of the vehicle fleet to cleaner and more efficient technologies.

  • TR.2. Efficient driving for public and freight transport.

  • TR.3. The increasing replacement of conventional vehicles by electric and hybrid vehicles.

  • TR.4. The H-green application. These last 2 measures are expected in an increasing percentage of penetration to the land vehicle fleet, ranging from 0.01% participation in tractor-trailers (electric or H-green) to 33.4% in public passenger transport ( with electric minibuses) by 2030.

    Based on the measures identified and their combination, 5 mitigation scenarios were built, with increasing levels of ambition:

The first consisted of the BAU Scenario (with conventional mixtures of biofuels, migration to fuel flex, from Liquefied Petroleum Gas (LPG) and naphtha), considering the measures already applied for its construction. In this scenario, modifications for river and air transport were not considered.

  • In Scenario 2, an increase in the percentage of diesel and biodiesel blends is considered, while the alcohol-gasoline blends maintain the same proportions as scenario 1. Furthermore, efficient driving / driving is considered to reduce consumption of fuels for land transport, specifically that of public passenger transport (minibuses and buses) and freight transport. In this scenario, modifications for land transport are considered.

  • In Scenario 3, in addition to considering the previous measures, the penetration of electric vehicles and H-green is considered. In this scenario, measures in land and river transport are already considered, and given their medium level of ambition, they are subsequently considered for the purposes of the construction of the national mitigation scenarios by 2030.

  • In Scenario 4, in addition to the measures applied in Scenario 3, the introduction of diesel rail transport to move loads over medium and long distances is considered. In addition, in road transport it is considered that from the year 2025 electric motorcycles will enter, reaching a penetration level of 20% of the total motorcycle fleet by 2050. This measure responds to the hypothesis that market forces, for Scenario 4, will be such that they will drive motorcycle users to opt for electric mobility.

  • In Scenario 5 and of greater ambition, the measures applied in Scenario 4 are considered, but with the difference that the rail transport for cargo mobilization will be electric. In addition, in road transport it is considered that from the year 2025 electric motorcycles will enter, reaching a penetration level of 30% of the total motorcycle fleet by 2050. This measure responds to the hypothesis that market forces, for Scenario 5, will be such that they will drive motorcycle users to opt for electric mobility, and a penetration percentage is considered higher than the previous scenario (30% ).

6. MEANS OF IMPLEMENTATION

The means of implementation constitute complementary and necessary elements to achieve the adaptation objectives and the mitigation measures identified in the update of the Nationally Determined Contribution.

Building and strengthening capacities (Art.11, Paris Agreement), climate finance (Art.9, Paris Agreement) and technology development, transfer and innovation (Art.10, Paris Agreement) are the three (3) fundamental pillars to advance towards the ambitious implementation of the NDC of Paraguay by 2030.

6.1 CONSTRUCTION AND STRENGTHENING OF CAPACITIES.

This means of implementation seeks to strengthen the processes of training, empowerment, awareness, research and transparency that allow consolidating existing technical capacities as well as building new capacities of key actors, decision makers, national technicians, academia and civil society.

Based on the needs detected, the following lines of action are proposed for 2025:

  • Public awareness and education for climate action.

  • Construction and strengthening of permanent technical skills in prioritized areas and sectors for the fulfillment of climate commitments.

  • Strengthening the institutional framework and empowerment for climate action through the design of training programs and continuous training on climate change, with an emphasis on compliance with the NDC of Paraguay.

  • Integration of the gender perspective and attention to vulnerable communities for the elaboration of climate policies, programs and action plans.

  • Articulation with academia and research centers at the national and international level to promote Research, Development and Innovation (R + D + I) related to climate action.

  • Promotion of international technical exchange regarding development, challenges and gaps for compliance with the NDC.

  • Training to strengthen the National Monitoring, Reporting and Verification (MRV) Systems as well as Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) to monitor the progress of the NDC.

  • Capacity-building for Judges, Court Members and operators of the different judicial districts of the country within the framework of the process of granting certificates according to the provisions of Article 12 of Law No. 3001/2006 “On the valuation and remuneration of the environmental services".

6.2 CLIMATE FINANCING

The objective of this means of implementation is to strengthen the management of financial resources allocated to climate change in such a way that they are aligned with the financing needs for the implementation of the NDC.

Next, the needs, progress and lines of action for 2025 are cited:

6.2.1 Climate financing needs.

  • Analysis of the costs associated with the full implementation of the NDC assumed by 2030.

  • Analysis of national public spending on climate change that allows cataloging activities and programs related to adaptation and mitigation in the General Budget of the Nation (PGN).

  • Official mechanism that can identify and evaluate climate financial flows for the consolidation of the National Monitoring, Reporting and Verification System for Transparency (SN-MRVT).

  • Development and access to financial mechanisms that allow the sustainability of the conservation units that make up the National System of Protected Wild Areas (SINASIP).

The above needs can be met by considering the following:

  • Have parameters to identify climate investments or expenditures in all national (public and private) and international economic sectors.

  • Expand the legal framework to regulate the registration of all national and international climate support received, including participation in voluntary carbon markets.

  • Position Paraguay before the Green Climate Fund (GCF), strengthening the Country Program, the methodology for evaluation, identification and prioritization of projects (portfolio), which must be aligned with public policies and international climate commitments, such as the National Development Plan Paraguay 2030 and the NDC.

6.2.2 Advances in climate finance

  • Through Law No. 5875/17, the National Climate Change Fund (FNCC) has been created whose purpose is to attract and apply public, private, national and international financial resources that support the implementation of actions to face climate change . This Law establishes that adaptation actions will be a priority in the application of the Fund and grants MADES exclusive competence to identify, obtain, plan, manage and apply the financial resources entered in the FNCC.

  • An Ad-Hoc Committee on Finance for Climate Change has been formed within the framework of the National Climate Change Commission with the objective of:

    • define the current scenario and the climate financing needs of the country;

    • work on the institutional arrangements necessary for the development of a mechanism that can identify, analyze and measure financial flows related to climate change;

    • expand the scope to develop strategies, raise funds / resources and seek financing mechanisms with a view to complying with the NDC of Paraguay.

  • Paraguay is developing its Third Biennial Update Report (IBA3) which will present improved information in order to be able to identify the destination of the funds (adaptation, mitigation, capacity building, technology, others) and the type of financial instrument ( donation, loan, guarantee, payment for results, etc.)

6.2.3 Lines of action to 2025.

Based on the needs identified and the progress made by the country, the following lines of action are proposed for 2025:

  • Estimate of the costs associated with the full implementation of the NDC including the Adaptation Communication.

  • Analysis of the private investment climate of the sectors prioritized in the NDC.

  • Preparation of the National Funding Strategy for the implementation of the NDC and other climate action initiatives.

  • Creation of incentives to promote and sustain private sector investment in climate change mitigation and adaptation actions.

  • Development of a financial mechanism that facilitates access to climate finance for the implementation of the NDC and other climate action initiatives.

6.3 DEVELOPMENT AND TRANSFER OF TECHNOLOGIES

Paraguay, in its NDC, proposes adaptation objectives and mitigation measures that require strong development, adoption and transfer of technologies.

Along these lines, the country is promoting mechanisms that promote technology transfer, which in turn facilitate research and analysis of appropriate technological solutions to reduce vulnerability and adapt to the adverse effects of climate change, including those that guarantee the poverty reduction, food and nutrition security (SAN).

In 2021, with the support of the Green Climate Fund (GCF) in Paraguay, the project “Guidance and technical support to carry out a Technological Needs Assessment (TNA) and a Technological Action Plan will begin. (TAP, for its acronym in English) ”that will help the country to assess priority technology needs and harmonize the development of the country towards a transition that contributes to achieving the objectives of the NDC in a viable, efficient and innovative way.

The timely evaluation of the priority technological needs and the construction of the Technological Action Plan, without a doubt, will be a fundamental pillar for the effective implementation of the measures and actions established in the update of the NDC.

The expected results are:

  • A Technology Needs Assessment and a Technology Action Plan to be reported to the UNFCCC.

  • A platform for the transfer and development of technologies available in the different state digital sites, including the creation of a platform for the exchange of information on the TNA and the progress in the implementation of the TAP.

  • Strengthening the capacities of all key actors in terms of development and transfer of technologies for climate action.

7.1.1 Improvements achieved in the Adaptation Component.

As illustrated in Figure 1, in the First NDC of Paraguay, adaptation was defined as a priority component for the country and the prioritized sectors were stated. The criterion for these prioritizations is vulnerability to climatic factors by the activities on which its economy is based (mainly in primary production). In addition to this, due to the condition of a landlocked country, trade is carried out across bordering borders using long transport services, both land and river (using rivers whose navigability, in turn, depends on climatic variability).

Since the ratification of the NDC to the present, the country has made great strides in providing a guiding framework, as well as offering tools to promote adaptation at the national and local levels. Thus, the National Strategy for Adaptation to Climate Change (2015) and its corresponding Implementation Plan were drawn up: National Plan for Adaptation to Climate Change (2017), in addition to the Guide for the Preparation of Local and Sectoral Plans for Adaptation to Climate Change (2018). These tools have promoted the development of Climate Action Plans in various local governments, as well as the National Plan for Disaster Risk Management and Adaptation to Climate Change in the Agricultural Sector of Paraguay (MAG / FAO, 2016).

In the present update of the NDC, Paraguay has managed to redefine the priority sectors (7), including those that are not only important for economic development, but also consider social, environmental and cultural aspects. On the other hand, these sectors establish objectives (25), with their lines of action and needs and gaps, identified in a broad participatory process (n = 481 participants), involving the Central, Local Government, Civil Society, Private Sector and the Academy. Additionally, the bases have been identified and established to build a National Monitoring and Evaluation System (M&E), which should be implemented in all sectors through institutional arrangements.

7.1.2 Improvements achieved in the Mitigation Component.

In this update, it has been possible to adjust the GHG emissions projections in the BAU (“Business as Usual” national and sectoral scenario according to various driver variables through historical data from the INGEI and projections built with econometric models ), as well as presenting mitigation scenarios, based on the 5 Sectoral Climate Change Mitigation Plans (PMCC).

Another improvement in the update of the First NDC of Paraguay is the presentation of the mitigation potential of the scenarios:

  • With measures: including mitigation measures "that are in application or have been approved" since 2014;

  • With additional measures: including "planned, approved and applied" mitigation measures. These advances lay the foundations for future revisions and / or updates, to be carried out within the framework of the First Biennial Transparency Report (IBT).

8.2 MONITORING, REPORTING AND VERIFICATION (MRV) OF THE MITIGATION COMPONENT

In order to follow the guidelines of the Reinforced Transparency Framework (MTR) and that the country has a solid National MRV System, necessary for accountability and in turn, for decision-making in its next NDC, it is presented then, as a preview, an analysis of the background and future proposals for its consolidation.

8.2.1. Background of a national proposal for MRV

The Second Biennial Update Report (IBA2 of MADES / UNDP / GEF, 2018) reported a proposal for an MRV system where the progress of the implementation of the different mitigation measures must be communicated and recorded in the National Climate Change System or Platform , currently constituted by the Environmental Information System (SIAM) of MADES. Said report proposed that the monitoring system at the national or domestic level include the following components:

  • MRV of GHG Emissions / Absorption: for the development of the National Inventories of Greenhouse Gases (INGEI), as well as other additional technical studies that are prepared within the framework of specific programs.

  • MRV of Mitigation Policies, Actions and Plans (PAM): relates to the monitoring of the progress of the measures, including Nationally Appropriate Mitigation Actions (NAMA) and Low Emission Development Strategies (known as LED for its acronym in English).

  • Implicit in this system is the MRV of REDD +: within the framework of the National Forest Monitoring System that contains information from the National Forest Inventory (IFN), the Satellite Terrestrial Monitoring System (SSMT) (carried out by INFONA, and with the quality control of MADES), the INGEI of the LULUCF Sector.

  • M&E of Adaptation Activities: implies the monitoring of the National Adaptation Plan (s) (NAP) where specific goals and indicators should be identified for follow-up according to the prioritized sectors.

  • MRV of the Support received and the Financing Needs: the component is based on the need to measure, report and verify the availability of the means of implementation. In the case of non-Annex I countries, such as Paraguay, it could also serve to identify financing needs;

Although progress has been made in recent years for each proposed component, it is still necessary to strengthen them and consider the necessary flexibility to adjust to national requirements and circumstances. Taking as a starting point the proposal for the existing national MRV system and the reporting and review requirements established by Decision 18 / CMA.1 for the Transparency Framework; the incorporation of the MRV of the NDC to the Mitigation Component is recommended, aiming at a "National System of Measurement, Monitoring and Verification for Transparency (SN-MRVT)" that will have as objective the management and adequate collection of all the necessary information the preparation of the “Biennial Transparency Reports” (BTR, for its acronym in English).

However, although the MRV system follows a sequential scheme, due to the principle of continuous improvement, it is not exempt from being a dynamic process, which requires constant feedback, which must be considered when implementing and giving follow-up to NDC goals.

In relation to the need for constant review of MRV systems, and in this particular case that of the NDC of the SN-MRVT, the following will be considered:

  • The revisions of the 5 PMCC, including the possibility of removal / inclusion of new PAMs that are relevant to the NDC;

  • The reviews of the sectoral and national projections (in particular of the projections “with measures”);

  • The recommendations of the external technical examination and,

  • Examples of good national and international practices.

Finally, as the Mitigation Component of the SN-MRVT must be able to monitor, report and verify the reduction of emissions associated with the implementation of the PMCC measures, it should be housed in the Climate Change Module of the SIAM (making use of of the specific sectoral MRV systems that exist in the country).
