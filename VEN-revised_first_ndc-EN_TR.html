1. INTRODUCTION

Climate Change is one of the biggest problems currently affecting the planet, this situation transcends the environmental and must be considered in its ethical, human, socio-economic and political dimensions. It is, in fact, one of the clearest manifestations of the crisis of capitalism as a predatory system of nature that makes a healthy, full, supportive and peaceful life on earth impossible.

It also translates into a dimension of the global environmental crisis generated by unsustainable patterns of production and consumption, particularly in developed countries, which have a historical responsibility in its generation. Therefore, only the modification of these patterns constitutes a true and lasting solution to the environmental crisis and consequently to the climate crisis.

The Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela, being a country characterized by presenting insular spaces and low altitude coasts, with arid and semi-arid zones exposed to floods, drought and desertification, and with fragile mountainous ecosystems; whose core economy is particularly dependent on the production, use and export of fossil fuels; It is considered, according to the categories established in the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC, 1992), as highly vulnerable; and particularly sensitive to the adverse effects of Climate Change and the secondary consequences of potential response measures.

This vulnerability has become evident in a series of emergencies and natural disasters with severe effects on housing, food production, hydroelectricity generation, and biological diversity, among others such as those that occurred during the months of August and September 2021 in the states of Mérida, Táchira and Anzoátegui.

Faced with this situation, the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela has taken large-scale measures with significant investments, which have made it possible to care for and protect the affected population to reduce damage and reduce vulnerability.

In the framework of what was agreed upon under the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change and the Paris Agreement (PA), Venezuela presents the update of the Nationally Determined Contribution (CND), committed to building a productive economic model ecosocialist. These actions are articulated within the framework of the Economic and Social Development Plan of the Nation Law of the Homeland Plan 2019 - 2025, where the deepening of ecosocialist policies with a high social, economic and environmental impact is proposed to contribute to the fight against Climate Change.

In the same way, said CND allows us to reaffirm the commitment that the country has in climate matters, in the different multilateral spaces of the United Nations system, as well as in the regional and inter-regional coordination mechanisms such as: The

Ibero-American Conference of Ministers of the Environment, the Forum of Ministers of the Environment of the United Nations Program for the Environment, Meeting of Ministers and High Authorities of the Environment of the Bolivarian Alliance for the peoples of Our America - Peoples' Trade Treaty (ALBA - TCP), the Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC), among others.

For this update process, the transparency guidelines emanating from the following Decisions 4/CMA.1, 1/CP.21 and 9/CMA.1 have been taken into account. Likewise, the recommendations of the instances and support organizations regarding elements considered essential to increase the level of ambition and the robustness of the information have been incorporated.

In this update and presentation of the CND, elements related to gender and equity are incorporated, which allow progress in the national objectives of the fight against poverty and inequality, within the framework of national planning for the achievement of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDG) and the 2030 Agenda. Likewise, the imposition of Unilateral Coercive Measures (MCU) that violate the human rights of the Venezuelan population, and the special situation due to the COVID-19 pandemic are incorporated into the elements. 19, which has required a rethinking and rearrangement of planning and State administration to face the critical situation and the post-2020 recovery scenario.

In this sense, Venezuela presents its contributions in adaptation and mitigation, encompassing actions and policies towards fair and sustainable development. These contributions have been advanced voluntarily and without international financing. They had not been updated, until now, due to the illegal application of the Unilateral Coercive Measures (MCU) that violate the human rights of the population and the health circumstance of the COVID-19 pandemic. Said update is framed in the Economic and Social Development Plan of the Nation, which establishes the priority of the fight against Climate Change within the framework of an integral, humanist and eco-socialist development oriented to Good Living in harmony with Mother Earth.

For each commitment, a brief description of the context is included with reference to the circumstances of the corresponding sector or strategic area, followed by a summary table that includes the commitment to its goal, the governing entity, the type of commitment, preliminary indicators and the SDGs to which they contribute. The description of each commitment is complemented by a brief indicative reference regarding the operational aspects related to the means of implementation, its potential for integration, adaptation - mitigation and culminating with a statement of long-term ambition.

In accordance with the provisions of the decisions under the Conference of the Parties to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) and its Paris Agreement, the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela presents here the update of its Nationally Determined Contribution (CND) for the fight against Climate Change and its effects.

This update of the Contribution must be examined in light of the principle of Common but Differentiated Responsibilities, which implies, among other elements, that in order to materialize the commitments expressed by developing countries, the financial resources provided for are required to support actions in developing countries, as well as technology transfer and capacity building, which developed countries must increasingly contribute, in accordance with their obligations established in the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC). ) and the Paris Agreement.

Finally, this update of the CND is part of a national process of collective construction of a proposed Law on Climate Change for the country, which involves different actors and sectors, organized communities and social movements, and which aspires to adopt in the legislative period from 2021 to 2026.

2. BACKGROUND

As part of the obligations assumed before the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela accepted the commitment to inform everything relevant to the contents, which by mandate of the Convention is established to the preparation of National Communications on Climate Change. Venezuela's political commitment is of a high level in the fight against Climate Change, as expressed by President Nicolás Maduro Moros, during the intervention at the 75th General Assembly of the United Nations Organization, held in October 2020. , namely:

We must all work for the salvation of our Planet. We call for the highest political commitment to combat climate change, which is a reality: to combat climate change as an urgent priority. We recognize the importance of strengthening mitigation and adaptation actions, in accordance with the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change and the Paris Agreement. We reaffirm, then, the importance of the means of implementation so that developing countries can carry out their climate actions. Noam Chomsky warned him recently: “there is not much time left”.

Reiterating in this way the great political commitment at the highest level in the fight against climate change.

2.1. THE UNITED NATIONS FRAMEWORK CONVENTION ON CLIMATE CHANGE (UNFCCC)

This instrument was approved in 1992 and ratified by Venezuela in 1994, through the Official Gazette of the Republic of Venezuela No. 4,825 Extraordinary of December 27, 1994.

2.2. THE KYOTO PROTOCOL

Approved and published in the Official Gazette of the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela No.

38,081 of December 7, 2004. This protocol establishes commitments and goals on emission reductions for Annex I countries, and voluntary reductions for Non-Annex I countries. In it, the Ministry with environmental competence, presented an Account Point to the President of the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela, in which the implementation of the Kyoto Protocol in our country is limited, specifically the sections related to the flexibility mechanisms provided for in this Protocol, called Clean Development Mechanisms (CDM) . This presidential decision not to resort to the Clean Development Mechanisms will later be reflected in the National Government Plans, First and Second Plan of the Homeland.

2.3. THE DOHA AMENDMENT TO THE KYOTO PROTOCOL

This amendment was approved at COP-18 in Qatar in 2012, and ratified by Venezuela in 2017, which establishes the subsequent commitment period for Annex I countries after 2012, and is increased to eight years the second period (2012 - 2020).

2.4. THE RATIFICATION OF THE PARIS AGREEMENT ON CLIMATE CHANGE

At COP-21 of the Conference of the Parties to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), Venezuela adopts the Paris Agreement on Climate Change on December 12, 2015.

In April 2016, Venezuela signed the Paris Agreement on Climate Change at the United Nations (UN) in the commemorative act of Earth Day, on April 22.

On July 21, 2017, Venezuela completes the steps required for the ratification of the Paris Agreement on Climate Change, with the deposit of the instrument of ratification before the depositary at the UN.

At that time, the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela becomes a State Party to the 154th Paris Agreement on Climate Change, in July 2017. The Constitution of the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela empowers the Executive to adopt international agreements, Article 236 on the Attributions of

President of the Republic: “4. Direct the foreign relations of the Republic and celebrate and ratify international treaties, conventions or agreements.”

2.5. THE LEGAL CONTEXT OF THE PARIS AGREEMENT ON CLIMATE CHANGE

The Paris Agreement on Climate Change is a legal instrument for the implementation of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), therefore the principles and provisions of the Convention prevail and guide the implementation of said agreement.

The previous work of elaboration and discussion of the Contribution planned and determined at the National level of Venezuela was advanced, by the then Ministry of Popular Power for Ecosocialism and Water (MINEA), in the previous years 2013 and 2014, today the Ministry of Popular Power for Ecosocialism (MINEC), compiling the contributions obtained through inter-ministerial consultations. Its delivery was formalized in the plenary for the adoption of the Paris Agreement at the XXI Conference of the Parties (COP-21), in December 2015.

In this order of ideas, Venezuela formalized its Nationally Determined Contribution (CND) before the Executive Secretariat of the CMUNCC, based in Bonn, Germany, and later formally delivered it in February 2018, before the Executive Secretariat of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change.

3. DESCRIPTION OF THE GENERAL OBJECTIVES OF THE CND - IMPLEMENTATION OF THE OBJECTIVES OF THE CONVENTION AND THE PARIS AGREEMENT

The Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela has a high political commitment, within the framework of the Paris Agreement, adopted through decision 1/CP.21, which addresses crucial areas necessary to combat climate change.

Therefore, the general objective of the NDCs for Venezuela is to implement policies, actions and efforts that promote the reduction of greenhouse gases and the increase in resilience and decrease in vulnerability to the adverse effects of climate change in the prioritized sectors. These actions and efforts will be directed through the strategic lines and measures identified in the subsequent sections of the document.

The Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela ratifies its commitment to reduce its GHG emissions by 20% by the year 2030 in relation to the inertial scenario. To this end, the country contemplates activities in various sectors, including adaptation and mitigation actions, with adaptation being the national priority. The degree to which this goal is achieved will depend on the fulfillment of the commitments of the developed countries regarding the provision of financing, technology transfer and capacity building in accordance with the provisions of Article 4.7 of the Convention.

In this sense, the country bases compliance with its CND, as a priority for the fight against poverty and its right to development, under the principles contemplated in the UNFCCC and other current and relevant international instruments, including the 2030 Agenda and the SDGs.

Ambitious implementation of NDCs is essential to ensure that near-future global emissions peaks and climate risks are reduced, so that development co-benefits can be simultaneously achieved. The Paris Agreement calls for each country to outline and communicate its post-2020 climate actions; and thus implement their determined contributions at the national level. Here are some of these key aspects:

  • Long-term temperature objective (article 2) The Paris Agreement, in seeking to strengthen the global response to climate change, reaffirms the objective of limiting the increase in global temperature very below 2°C, while efforts continue to limit it to 1.5°C.

  • Peak and climate neutrality (Article 4) In order to achieve this temperature target, the Parties aim to reach the peak of global GHG emissions as soon as possible, and to recognize that that point maximum will take more time for developing country Parties to achieve a balance between anthropogenic emissions by sources and removals by sinks of GHGs in the second half of the century.

  • Mitigation (Article 4) The Paris Agreement establishes binding commitments by all Parties to prepare, communicate and maintain a CND and apply national measures to achieve them. It also establishes that the Parties will communicate their nationally determined contributions every five years, and will provide the necessary information for clarity and transparency. To set a firm foundation for higher ambition, each successive Nationally Determined Contribution will represent a progression beyond the previous one, and will reflect the highest possible ambition. Developed countries should continue to take the lead by setting absolute economy-wide reduction targets, while developing countries should continue to intensify their mitigation efforts, while being encouraged to move towards achieving the economy-wide targets. economy over time, in light of different national circumstances.

  • Sinks and reservoirs (Article 5) -The Paris Agreement also encourages Parties to conserve and enhance, as appropriate, the GHG sinks and reservoirs referred to in paragraph d ) of Article 4, paragraph 1, of the Convention, including forests.

  • Voluntary Participation / Market and Non-Market Approaches (Article 6) The Paris Agreement recognizes the possibility of voluntary participation between Parties to enable greater ambition and establishes principles - including integrity transparency, and sound accounting - for any cooperation that involves the international transfer of mitigation results. It establishes a mechanism to contribute to the mitigation of GHG emissions and support sustainable development, and defines a framework for non-market approaches to sustainable development.

  • Adaptation (article 7). The Paris Agreement sets a global goal on adaptation, namely increasing adaptive capacity, strengthening resilience and reducing vulnerability to climate change in the context of the Agreement's temperature target. Its goal is to significantly strengthen national adaptation efforts, including through international support and cooperation. The Agreement recognizes that adaptation is a global challenge facing everyone. All Parties should engage in adaptation, including through the development and implementation of national adaptation plans, and should submit and regularly update an adaptation communication outlining their priorities, needs, plans and actions. The adaptation efforts of developing countries should be recognized.

  • Loss and damage (Article 8) The Paris Agreement recognizes the importance of avoiding, minimizing and addressing loss and damage related to the adverse effects of climate change , including extreme weather events and slow onset events, and the contribution of sustainable development to reducing the risk of loss and damage. Parties should strengthen understanding, action and support, including through the Warsaw International Mechanism for Loss and Damage, in a cooperative and facilitative manner with respect to loss and damage related to the adverse effects of climate change.

  • Financial, technological and capacity-building resources (Articles 9, 10 and 11) The Paris Agreement reaffirms the obligations of developed countries to support the efforts of the Parties that are developing countries to build a clean and climate-resilient future, while for the first time encouraging other Parties to provide or continue to provide such support on a voluntary basis.

Resource provision should also aim to strike a balance between adaptation and mitigation. In addition to reporting on funding already provided, developed country Parties undertake to provide information

transparent and indicative information on future support for developing country parties, including expected levels of public funding.

The Paris Agreement also provides for the Financial Mechanism of the Convention, including the Green Climate Fund, which will serve the Agreement. International cooperation in the development and transfer of climate technology is also strengthened, as well as the strengthening of institutional capacities in the Parties of the developing world through the necessary ad hoc institutional agreements.

  • Education, training, public awareness and participation and public access to information on climate change (Article 12). They should also be strengthened under the Agreement.

  • Transparency (article 14), implementation and compliance (article 15). The Paris Agreement is based on a robust system of transparency and accountability to provide clarity on actions and support by Parties, with flexibility for the different capacities of Parties. In addition to submitting information on mitigation, adaptation and support, the Agreement requires that the information submitted by each Party be subject to an international review by technical experts. The Agreement also includes a mechanism that will facilitate enforcement and promote compliance in a non-adversarial and non-punitive manner, and will report annually to the CP/RA.

  • World balance (article 14). A "global stocktake", which will take place in 2023 and every five years thereafter, will assess collective progress towards achieving the Agreement's goals in a comprehensive and facilitative way. It will be based on the best available science and its long-term global goal. Its result will serve as a basis for the Parties to update and improve their measures and support and increase international cooperation in the fight against climate change.

10.2.3. Forest Sector (General Directorate of Forest Heritage - Misión Árbol Foundation)

The country's forest heritage covers all the ecosystems and forest resources included in the national territory, including natural forests, forest plantations, trees outside the forest, as well as forest lands and associated non-tree vegetation forms or not to the forest.

Much of the surface of Venezuela is covered by an enormous variety of tropical forests, very complex and biodiverse mosaics, which vary from forests just a few meters high and with a single layer, to large, multi-layered forests , between 40-50 m or more in height; There are also forests dominated by practically a single species (contemporary), alongside forests with 150 to 200 tree species per hectare (heteroetaneous). Forests in Venezuela tend to grow in all terrestrial and floodplain environments of the low, medium, and highlands, up to an approximate elevation of 3,000 m asl in the Andes region (the vast majority between 0 and 2,500 m asl, that is, , the macrothermal and mesothermal zones of the country). Even above this general limit of trees, there is another type of forest, the chiribital, which forms very isolated and small tree communities in surface area and in heights up to 3,800 / 4,200 m asl

Among the main forest formations of Venezuela we have: humid forests, dry forests, cloud forests, evergreen forests, mangrove forests and palm forests.

In 2014, the Ministry of People's Power for the Environment, today MINEC, published the vegetation cover map of Venezuela, at a scale of 1:2,000,000, presenting the updated cartography of the country's vegetation cover, in 75 letters on a scale of 1:250,000.

A good proportion of this Venezuelan forest heritage is under the ABRAE category, which represents 67.52% of the total land area of the country, where the functions of protection and conservation of biological diversity assigned to forests represent 37.26%; the function of forest production under management plans represents 17.7% and the remaining 19.11% is given by the multiple use function of the forest. This protection condition includes the country among the ten nations with the largest extension of forests designated for the protection of soil and water (FAO, 2016).

Deforestation in Venezuela, for the decade 1990-2000 was 288,000 ha/year and for the following decade 2000-2010 it was 164,600 ha/year, reducing the loss of forest cover by 42.9% (MPPA, 2010).

Forest plantations for commercial purposes have been developed mainly by the public sector with a participation of 72.7%; only 27.24 corresponds to the private sector. By 2013, the planted forest area reached 557,000 ha (Forest Statistics, 2009 - 2013. Ministry of People's Power for the Environment).

11. GUIDELINE 2. ADAPTATION

Article 4 of the Framework Convention establishes that the Parties must formulate, apply, publish and regularly update national and, as appropriate, regional programs that contain (...) measures to facilitate adequate adaptation to climate change.

With the Paris Agreement, the adaptation approach is strengthened; specifically in its article 2, literal b, which raises the need to increase the capacity to adapt to the adverse effects of climate change and promote climate resilience and development with low GHG emissions; Similarly, Article 7 establishes a global adaptation goal, recognizing that the current need for adaptation is considerable, and that a lower level of mitigation will increase the need for additional adaptation efforts.

Both legal frameworks on climate change (UNFCCC and the Paris Agreement) guide the preparation and content of the Adaptation Communication.

The Report of Group I on the Physical Basis of Climate Change (2021), from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), concludes that warming in the climate system is a reality and that changes are being observed that they are unprecedented. Significant changes in weather variables, particularly precipitation and temperature, jeopardize the normal development of society in many countries, and especially the low-income population of so-called developing countries, who will suffer the most negative effects due to its greater vulnerability.

In September 2015, the UN approved the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, together with an Agenda that includes 17 SDGs and 169 goals to be achieved by countries in the period 2016-2030 in order to move towards a more sustainable world. sustainable. Goal 13 of the SDGs is aimed at: "Adopt urgent measures against climate change".

In turn, this objective presents five goals that include, among others: Strengthen resilience and adaptive capacity to risks related to climate and natural disasters in all countries; incorporate climate change measures into national policies, strategies and plans; and improve education, awareness, and human and institutional capacity in relation to climate change mitigation, adaptation, impact reduction, and early warning.

Article 127 of the Constitution of the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela (CRBV) determines that the enjoyment of a safe, healthy and ecologically balanced life and environment is an individual and collective right. Likewise, the CRBV article 55 prescribes that

…everyone has the right to protection by the State through citizen security bodies regulated by law, in the face of situations that constitute a threat, vulnerability or risk to the physical integrity of people, their property, the enjoyment of their rights and the fulfillment of their duties.”

With respect to the legislation that may be relevant to the processes of adaptation to climate change, the Organic Law of the Environment of 2006, in its Article 23 includes as part of the guidelines for environmental planning the insertion in them of risk prevention systems.

In terms of law, Venezuela has a substantial legal and regulatory framework that regulates the implementation of policies, programs, plans, and projects, and bases the actions of the State aimed at adapting to the effects of climate change, to face the conditions of vulnerability.

In the first instance, there is the Plan for the Homeland 2019 - 2025, now converted into law, which develops five historical objectives, being the 5th Historical Objective: Contribute to the preservation of life on the planet and the salvation of the species which expresses the guidelines and mandates related to the environmental issue and specifically to climate change in the national objective 5.4.

Likewise, in Venezuela the disaster prevention and care guidelines of various international bodies have been adopted, such as the International Strategy for Disaster Reduction - EIRD.

In the framework of the ISDR, Venezuela participated in the World Conference on Disaster Reduction, held in 2005 in Kobe - Japan. In said event, the Hyogo Framework for Action - MAH, 2005 - 2015 was adopted, whose general objective is to increase the resilience of nations and communities in the face of disasters by achieving, by 2015, a considerable reduction in losses caused by disasters, both in terms of human lives and in terms of the social, economic and environmental assets of communities and countries.

From the perspective of shared but differentiated responsibilities, Venezuela has been developing State policies in correlative actions to adaptation to Climate Change and the commitments assumed in the framework of the UNFCCC.

The agricultural sector is one of those that presents the greatest risk in the face of climate change, coupled with anthropogenic processes, the more drastic the climate changes, the greater the challenge for farmers or producers, in terms of the conditions for the production and benefits derived from the activity.

However, a large part of the greenhouse gases worldwide come from this sector, mainly due to the intensive use of synthetic fertilizers and pesticides, the digestive process of ruminants, the excessive use of fossil energy and the management and use Inadequate soil (erosion, contamination), therefore agricultural activity has a great responsibility in climate change, but it is also one of the most affected by it.

Various alternatives have been generated worldwide to remedy this situation. Agroecology emerges as a type of alternative and ancestral agriculture, to face conventional practices, by promoting sustainable practices such as the production and use of bio-inputs, cover crops, as well as the combination between crops and livestock, agroforestry, among others, that allow greater carbon sequestration; in it, the materialism of conventional agriculture is replaced by a vision that is more respectful of the environment and seeks to coexist with the environment without harming it, and as an added value to minimize environmental impact and food safety.

It is important to remember that climate change affects the phenology patterns of crops and yields, which is why the Ministry of People's Power for Productive Agriculture and Land (MPPAPT), through the National Institute of Comprehensive Agricultural Health (INSAI) ), its bioinputs laboratories provide farmers with biological agricultural inputs, in order to reduce the toxic load and carbon footprint, reduce production costs and have more resilient and sustainable systems.

11.3.7. Basic Education Sector

Environmental problems and disasters have become one of the greatest political, economic, scientific and educational concerns worldwide, on whose solution depends, to a large extent, the existence of life on earth. Likewise, the Organic Law of the Environment (2006) defines Environmental Education as the continuous, interactive and integrating process, through which the human being acquires knowledge and experiences, understands and analyzes them, internalizes them and translates them into behaviors, values and attitudes that prepare them to take a leading role in environmental management and sustainable development (art. 3); which aims to promote, generate, and consolidate in citizens knowledge, skills and attitudes to contribute to the transformation of society, which will be reflected in alternative solutions to socio-environmental problems, thus contributing to the achievement of well-being social, integrating into the management of the environment through active and leading participation, under the premise of sustainable development (art.34).

Similarly, the Organic Law of Education of 2009 contemplates as one of the purposes of education to promote the formation of an ecological conscience to preserve biodiversity and socio-diversity, environmental conditions and the rational use of natural resources (art. 15.5).

Since 2007, the Ministry of Popular Power for Education (MPPE), responding to the need to strengthen the educational system, has prepared a curricular proposal that contains theoretical orientations, objectives, integrating axes, and profiles of students and teachers , in addition to the functional orientations (relationships in practice between teachers, learning areas, curricula, learning experiences and evaluation).

Among the integrating axes of the Bolivarian National Curriculum of the Basic Education Subsystem, we have "Environment and Integral Health" aimed at promoting the development of harmonious relationships between human beings and nature (environment), highlighting the importance of seeing the whole , Planet Earth as an integrated system; where human beings are immersed; where we must learn to take advantage of and make rational and conscious use of natural resources.

In addition, it promotes the understanding of comprehensive health, from all aspects (sexual reproduction, risk management, recreation, nutrition, among others), thus minimizing threat scenarios, natural physical risks and social impacts, which lead to the strengthening of a culture of prevention, contributing to the full development of the human being and living well.

11.3.18. Biodiversity Sector (Conservation and Sustainable Use of Biological Diversity)

Climate change has a clear impact on biodiversity. This is constituted as a threat to individuals, species and ecosystems; on the other hand, the affectations of biodiversity can alter the structure and functioning of ecosystems, and their interactions with biological, geochemical and hydrological cycles (IPCC, 2007).

A determining factor is any natural or anthropogenic factor that causes, directly or indirectly, a change in an ecosystem or in biodiversity. In those that influence explicitly they are direct determinants. Among them are changes in land use, climate change, the presence of invasive species, the overexploitation of natural resources and pollution.

It should be noted that indirect determinants operate in a diffuse manner, affecting one or several determinants. Among the indirect factors that affect biodiversity are changes in human populations, changes in economic activity, demographic, socio-political, cultural and religious changes; and scientific and technological change (MEA, 2005).

Faced with this situation, the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela has taken large-scale measures that have made it possible to care for and protect the affected population, reduce damage, and reduce vulnerability. 12. SUPPORT AND IMPLEMENTATION NEEDS

Climate Change and its effects, as well as to increase the resilience of its natural and human systems.

Access to climate financing represents a challenge for the country, which is currently subjected to the cruelest Unilateral Coercive Measures (MCU) that violate the fundamental Human Rights of the population. Said financing is considered important for the achievement of the objectives of this CND and to contribute to the achievement of the SDGs.

The implementation of the Updated CND projects challenges in terms of financing, technical, technological, scientific capacities, institutional and governance transformation. Although there are initiatives and alliances in the country between the different institutions of the public and private sectors, academia and non-governmental organizations, capacity building and access to non-reimbursable financial resources from international cooperation are required for compliance.

It is important to point out that an assessment of support and implementation needs is required in accordance with the guidelines and methodologies, which allow quantifying the needs for the execution of adaptation and mitigation measures to climate change framed in the Paris Agreement and in the UNFCCC.

Some of the barriers and challenges for Venezuela, in relation to the implementation of adaptation, mitigation and losses and damages due to climate change revolve around implementation at the sectoral, territorial and local level, among which the following stand out:

  1. Update the legal framework to integrate climate change into future development planning.

  2. Update the available climate projections and low territorial coverage related to the analysis of vulnerability to the effects of climate change.

  3. Expand training and capacities to develop climate risk analysis (under the conceptual and methodological framework of the IPCC) at the sectorial, territorial and local level.

  4. Update information on the impacts of climate change on the marine-coastal region, mountain areas, and the country's biological diversity, among others.

ANNEX: PROCESS FOR UPDATING THE SECOND NATIONALLY DETERMINED CONTRIBUTION (CND)

The update of the Nationally Determined Contribution (CND) of the Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela was carried out through a broad, systematic and multisectoral participatory process for the joint construction and validation of sectoral commitments, Led by MINEC through the General Directorate for Climate Change Mitigation and Adaptation, as Technical Focal Point before the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC).

In order to ensure the participation of all the key sectors of Venezuela in the update of the second CND, the MINEC designed an action plan with execution activities in the years 2020-2021; carrying out the following actions, namely:

  • A Nationally Determined Contributions (NDC) Workshop was held in the context of unilateral coercive measures and SARS-CoV-2 (severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2, Coronavirus type 2 of the severe acute respiratory syndrome) or COVID 19, represent for Venezuela to respond in this context to the commitments assumed for the reduction of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and adaptation to climate change, in accordance with the provisions of the Convention United Nations Framework on Climate Change (UNFCCC) and the 2015 Paris Agreement.

  • Inter-institutional coordination and articulation with the different sectors.

  • Inter-institutional link was established for the collection of information and interaction with each sector.

  • 26 work meetings were established with external sectors and 11 work meetings with the internal substantive Directorates of Minec, which allowed a thorough review of the needs and barriers to be overcome for the implementation of the CNDs. It is important to note that, in compliance with the mobility and biosafety restriction measures due to the health crisis caused by the COVID-19 pandemic, the sessions were mostly held online.

  • Raising the CNDs in the different sectors and advancing in their implementation.

The process was coordinated by the Management team of the General Directorate for Climate Change Adaptation and Mitigation as speakers/facilitators, and had the methodological support and guidance derived from international forums and workshops in which our country in virtual mode.

The collaborative work consisted of: (1) bibliographic review and analysis of political and national development priorities; (2) preliminary preparation of possible commitments on climate change and their prioritization; (3) successive work meetings of the technical teams until a consensus is reached regarding the scope, structure, follow-up mechanisms, among other operational aspects of each commitment; (4) various consultations via telephone, email, via zoom for each sector to expand actions to address the climate crisis; (5) space for socialization and feedback through meetings; (6) technical-political approval process for all parties involved.
